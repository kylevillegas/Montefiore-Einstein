911<section id="_304">

912<h3 id="_304_toc">

913Follow-up and Survivorship</h3>

914<p id="_2" tabindex="-1">

915Renal cell cancer, also called renal adenocarcinoma or hypernephroma, can

916often be cured if it is diagnosed and treated when still localized to the

917kidney and the immediately surrounding tissue. The probability of cure is

918directly related to the stage or degree of tumor dissemination. Even when

919regional lymphatics or blood vessels are involved with tumor, a significant

920number of patients can achieve prolonged survival and probable cure.[<a href="#cit/section_1.2">

9212</a>

922] When

923distant metastases are present, disease-free survival is poor; however,

924some patients will survive after surgical resection of all known

925tumor. Because most patients are diagnosed when the tumor is still

926relatively localized and amenable to surgical removal, approximately 75% of all

927patients with renal cell cancer survive for 5 years.[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

9283</a>

929] Occasionally, patients with locally

930advanced or metastatic disease may exhibit indolent courses lasting several

931years. Late tumor recurrence many years after initial treatment also occasionally

932occurs.

933</p>

934<p id="_3" tabindex="-1">

935Renal cell cancer is one of the few tumors in which well-documented cases of

936spontaneous tumor regression in the absence of therapy exist, but this regression occurs

937rarely and may not lead to long-term survival. </p>

938</section>

939<section id="_305">

940<h3 id="_305_toc">

941Treatment Modalities</h3>

942<p id="_306" tabindex="-1">

943Surgical resection is the

944mainstay of treatment of this disease. Even in patients with disseminated

945tumor, locoregional forms of therapy may play an important role in palliating

946symptoms of the primary tumor or of ectopic hormone production. Systemic

947therapy has demonstrated only limited effectiveness.

948</p>

949</section>

972 <section id="_5">

973

974 <h2>

975Cellular Classification of Renal Cell Cancer</h2>

976

977 <div id="_section_5" class="pdq-sections">

978<p id="_6" tabindex="-1">

979Approximately 85% of renal cell cancers are adenocarcinomas, mostly

980of proximal tubular origin. Most of the remainder are transitional cell

981carcinomas of the renal pelvis. For more information, see <a href="/types/kidney/hp/transitional-cell-treatment-pdq">

982Transitional Cell

983Cancer of the Renal Pelvis and Ureter Treatment</a>

984.

985Adenocarcinomas may be separated into clear cell and granular cell carcinomas; however, the two cell types may occur together in some tumors. Some

986investigators have found that granular cell tumors have a worse prognosis, but

987this finding is not universal. Distinguishing between well-differentiated

988renal adenocarcinomas and renal adenomas can be difficult. The diagnosis is

989usually made arbitrarily on the basis of the size of the mass, but size alone

990should not influence the treatment approach because metastases can occur with

991lesions as small as 0.5 centimeter.

992</p>

1003

1004 <div id="_section_7" class="pdq-sections">

1015<p id="_8" tabindex="-1">

1016The staging system for renal cell cancer is based on the degree of tumor spread

1017beyond the kidney.[<a href="#cit/section_3.1">

10181</a>

1019-<a href="#cit/section_3.3">

10203</a>

1021] Involvement of blood vessels may not be a poor

1022prognostic sign if the tumor is otherwise confined to the substance of the

1023kidney. Abnormal liver function test results may be caused by a paraneoplastic

1024syndrome that is reversible with tumor removal, and these types of results do not necessarily represent

1025metastatic disease. Except when computed tomography (CT) examination is

1026equivocal or when iodinated contrast material is contraindicated, CT scanning

1027is as good as or better than magnetic resonance imaging for detecting

1028renal masses.[<a href="#cit/section_3.4">

10294</a>

1030]

1031</p>

1570<p id="_161" tabindex="-1">

1571Surgical resection is the accepted, often curative, therapy for patients with stage I renal

1572cell cancer. Resection may be simple or radical. Radical resection

1573includes removal of the kidney, adrenal gland, perirenal fat, and Gerota's

1574fascia, with or without a regional lymph node dissection. Some, but not all,

1575surgeons believe radical surgery yields superior results. </p>

1576<p id="_514" tabindex="-1">

1577In patients with bilateral stage I

1578neoplasms (concurrent or subsequent), bilateral partial nephrectomy or

1579unilateral partial nephrectomy with contralateral radical nephrectomy, when

1580technically feasible, may be a preferred alternative to bilateral nephrectomy

1581with dialysis or transplantation.[<a href="#cit/section_5.2">

15822</a>

1583] Increasing evidence suggests that a

1584partial nephrectomy is curative in selected cases. A

1585pathologist should examine the gross specimen as well as the frozen section from the

1586parenchymal margin of excision.[<a href="#cit/section_5.4">

15874</a>

1588]</p>

1589<p id="_515" tabindex="-1">

1590In patients

1591who are not candidates for surgery, EBRT or arterial

1592embolization can provide palliation.</p>

2345<h3 id="_623_toc">

2346Immunotherapy</h3>

2347<section id="_624">

2348<h4 id="_624_toc">

2349Immune checkpoint inhibitors</h4>

2350<p id="_625" tabindex="-1">

2351Immune checkpoint inhibitors are drugs that block certain proteins that inhibit the immune system's response to cancer. These proteins down-regulate T-lymphocyte activity and can prevent these cells from killing cancer cells. By reducing the activity of these inhibitory proteins, immune checkpoint inhibitors increase the immune response to cancer. Immune checkpoint proteins that are targeted by this class of drugs include programmed death-1 (PD-1), programmed cell death-ligand-1 (PD-L1), and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4).</p>

23571</a>

2731<h3 id="_183_toc">

2732Antiangiogenic and Other Targeted Therapy</h3>

2733<p id="_267" tabindex="-1">

2734A growing understanding of the biology of cancer in general, and renal cell cancer in particular, has led to the development and U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval of six new agents that target specific growth pathways. Two of the approved targeted therapies block the mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR), a serine/threonine protein kinase that regulates cell growth, division, and survival. </p>

2735<section id="_313">

2736<h4 id="_313_toc">

2737Anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and multitargeted tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs)</h4>

2738<p id="_314" tabindex="-1">

2739Based on research showing that most clear cell renal cell carcinomas carried a mutation resulting in constitutive production of cytokines stimulating angiogenesis, several agents that targeted VEGF-mediated pathways were developed. Several of these agents have been shown in randomized, controlled trials to significantly delay progression of clear cell renal cell carcinoma, but none has resulted in a statistically significant increase in OS as conventionally assessed. Many of these trials allowed crossover upon progression and, in some instances, other agents with similar biological activity were available to patients after they withdrew from the clinical trial. These circumstances may have made it more difficult to detect an OS benefit. For the clinician, this makes it challenging to determine the real benefit of these drugs to the patient. The four FDA-approved anti-VEGF agents include three oral TKIs: pazopanib, sorafenib, and sunitinib; and an anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody, bevacizumab. Axitinib is a newer, highly selective, and more potent inhibitor of VEGF receptors 1, 2, and 3. The FDA approved axitinib for the treatment of advanced renal cell carcinoma after the failure of one previously received systemic therapy.[<a href="#cit/section_8.52">

274052</a>

2741]</p>
