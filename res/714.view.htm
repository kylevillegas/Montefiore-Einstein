497

498 <p>

499Chemotherapy can cause side effects</p>

500

501</h2>

502

503<p>

504Chemotherapy not only kills fast-growing cancer cells, but also kills or slows the growth of healthy cells that grow and divide quickly. Examples are cells that line your mouth and <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046335&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000046335&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

505intestines</a>

506 and those that cause your hair to grow. Damage to healthy cells may cause side effects, such as mouth sores, nausea, and hair loss. Side effects often get better or go away after you have finished chemotherapy.</p>

507

508

509<p>

510The most common side effect is fatigue, which is feeling exhausted and worn out. You can prepare for fatigue by</p>

511

512

513<ul>

514<li>

515asking someone to drive you to and from chemotherapy</li>

516

517 <li>

518planning time to rest on the day of and day after chemotherapy</li>

519

520 <li>

521asking for help with meals and childcare on the day of and at least one day after chemotherapy</li>

522

523</ul>

524<p>

525There are many ways you can help manage chemotherapy side effects. For more information, see the section on <a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="e7202b67-b320-4e4e-bdfe-bd307e7cba5e" href="/about-cancer/treatment/side-effects">

526side effects</a>

527.</p>

577 <h2 id="what-to-expect-when-receiving-chemotherapy">

578

579 <p>

580What to expect when receiving chemotherapy</p>

581

582</h2>

583

584<h3>

585How chemotherapy is given</h3>

586

587

588<p>

589Chemotherapy may be given in many ways. Some common ways include</p>

590

591

592<ul>

593<li>

594<b>

595oral: </b>

596comes in pills, capsules, or liquids that you swallow</li>

597

598 <li>

599<b>

600intravenous (IV): </b>

601goes directly into a <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000476471" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000476471&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

602vein</a>

603</li>

604

605 <li>

606<b>

607injection: </b>

608given by a shot in a muscle in your arm, thigh, or hip, or right under the skin in the fatty part of your arm, leg, or belly</li>

609

610 <li>

611<b>

612intrathecal: </b>

613<a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000044678" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000044678&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

614injected</a>

615 into the space between the layers of tissue that cover the brain and spinal cord</li>

616

617 <li>

618<b>

619intraperitoneal (IP): </b>

620goes directly into the peritoneal cavity, which is the area in your body that contains <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000257523" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000257523&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

621organs</a>

622 such as your intestines, stomach, and liver</li>

623

624 <li>

625<b>

626intra-arterial (IA): </b>

627injected directly into the <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000440100" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000440100&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

628artery</a>

629 that leads to the cancer</li>

630

631 <li>

632<b>

633topical:</b>

634 comes in a cream that you rub onto your skin</li>

635

636</ul>

637<p>

638Of all the methods mentioned above, chemotherapy is most often given with an IV, through a thin needle that is placed in a vein on your hand or lower arm. Your nurse will put the needle in at the start of each treatment and remove it when treatment is over. IV chemotherapy may also be given through catheters or ports, sometimes with the help of a pump.</p>

639

640

641<ul>

642<li>

643<b>

644Catheter:</b>

645 A catheter is a thin, soft tube. A doctor or nurse places one end of the catheter in a large vein, often in your chest area. The other end of the catheter stays outside your body. Most catheters stay in place until you have finished your chemotherapy treatments. Catheters can also be used to give you other drugs and to draw blood. Be sure to watch for signs of infection around your catheter. See the section about <a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="6c4921fd-ebe3-4a3e-a9d4-027b2b6d7c38" href="/about-cancer/treatment/side-effects/infection">

646infection</a>

647 for more information.</li>

648

649 <li>

650<b>

651Port: </b>

652A port is a small, round disc that is placed under your skin during minor surgery. A <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000044306" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000044306&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

653surgeon</a>

654 puts it in place before you begin your course of treatment, and it remains there until you have finished. A catheter connects the port to a large vein, most often in your chest. Your nurse can insert a needle into your port to give you chemotherapy or draw blood. This needle can be left in place for chemotherapy treatments that are given for longer than one day. Be sure to watch for signs of infection around your port. See the section about <a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="6c4921fd-ebe3-4a3e-a9d4-027b2b6d7c38" href="/about-cancer/treatment/side-effects/infection">

655infection</a>

656 for more information.</li>

657

658 <li>

659<b>

660Pump: </b>

661Pumps are often attached to catheters or ports. They control how much and how fast chemotherapy goes into a catheter or port, allowing you to receive your chemotherapy outside of the hospital. Pumps can be internal or external. External pumps remain outside your body. Internal pumps are placed under your skin during surgery.</li>

662

663</ul>

664<h3>

665How your doctor decides which chemotherapy drugs to give you</h3>

666

667

668<p>

669There are many different chemotherapy drugs. Which ones are included in your treatment plan depends mostly on</p>

670

671

672<ul>

673<li>

674the type of cancer you have and how advanced it is</li>

675

676 <li>

677whether you have had chemotherapy before</li>

678

679 <li>

680whether you have other health problems, such as <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000044911" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000044911&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

681diabetes</a>

682 or <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000748138" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000748138&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

683heart disease</a>

684</li>

685

686</ul>

687<h3>

688Where you go for chemotherapy</h3>

689

690

691<p>

692You may receive chemotherapy during a hospital stay, at home, or as an outpatient at a doctor’s office, clinic, or hospital. Outpatient means you do not stay overnight. No matter where you go for chemotherapy, your doctor and nurse will watch for side effects and help you manage them. For more information on side effects and how to manage them, see the section on <a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="e7202b67-b320-4e4e-bdfe-bd307e7cba5e" href="/about-cancer/treatment/side-effects">

693side effects</a>

694.</p>

695

696

697<h3>

698How often you receive chemotherapy</h3>

699

700

701<p>

702Treatment schedules for chemotherapy vary widely. How often and how long you get chemotherapy depends on</p>

703

704

705<ul>

706<li>

707your type of cancer and how advanced it is</li>

708

709 <li>

710whether chemotherapy is used to

711 <ul style="list-style-type: circle;">

712<li>

713cure your cancer</li>

714

715 <li>

716control cancer's growth</li>

717

718 <li>

719ease symptoms</li>

720

721 </ul>

722</li>

723

724 <li>

725the type of chemotherapy you are getting</li>

726

727 <li>

728how your body responds to the chemotherapy</li>

729

730</ul>

731<p>

732You may receive chemotherapy in cycles. A cycle is a period of chemotherapy treatment followed by a period of rest. For instance, you might receive chemotherapy every day for 1 week followed by 3 weeks with no chemotherapy. These 4 weeks make up one cycle. The rest period gives your body a chance to <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000454708" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000454708&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

733recover</a>

734 and build new healthy cells.</p>

735

736

737<h3>

738Missing a chemotherapy treatment</h3>

739

740

741<p>

742It is best not to skip a chemotherapy treatment. But, sometimes your doctor may change your chemotherapy schedule if you are having certain side effects. If this happens, your doctor or nurse will explain what to do and when to start treatment again.</p>

745<h3>

746How chemotherapy may affect you</h3>

747

748

749<p>

750Chemotherapy affects people in different ways. How you feel depends on</p>

751

752

753<ul>

754<li>

755the type of chemotherapy you are getting</li>

756

757 <li>

758the dose of chemotherapy you are getting</li>

759

760 <li>

761your type of cancer</li>

762

763 <li>

764how advanced your cancer is</li>

765

766 <li>

767how healthy you are before treatment</li>

768

769</ul>

770<p>

771Since everyone is different and people respond to chemotherapy in different ways, your doctor and nurses cannot know for sure how you will feel during chemotherapy.</p>

772

773

774<h3>

775How will I know if chemotherapy is working?</h3>

776

777

778<p>

779You will see your doctor often. During these visits, they will ask you how you feel, do a <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000270871" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000270871&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

780physical exam</a>

781, and order medical tests and <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000046570" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046570&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

782scans</a>

783. Tests might include blood tests. Scans might include <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000045788" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000045788&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

784MRI</a>

785, <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000046033" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046033&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English">

786CT</a>

787, or <a class="definition" data-glossary-id="CDR0000046140" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046140&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=en">

788PET scans</a>

789.</p>

790

791

792<p>

793You cannot tell if chemotherapy is working based on its side effects. Some people think that severe side effects mean that chemotherapy is working well, or that no side effects mean that chemotherapy is not working. The truth is that side effects have nothing to do with how well chemotherapy is fighting your cancer.</p>

826Working during chemotherapy</p>

828</h2>

830<p>

831Many people can work during chemotherapy, as long as they match their work schedule to how they feel. Whether or not you can work may depend on what kind of job you have. If your job allows, you may want to see if you can work part-time or from home on days you do not feel well.</p>
