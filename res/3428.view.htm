928 <h2 id="what-is-genetic-testing" itemprop="name">

929

930 <p>

931What is genetic testing?</p>

932

933</h2>

934

935 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

936

937 <div itemprop="text">

938

939<p>

940Genetic testing looks for specific <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000045098&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000045098&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

941inherited</a>

942 changes (variants) in a person’s <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000045693&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000045693&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

943genes</a>

944. Genetic variants can have harmful, beneficial, neutral (no effect), or unknown or uncertain effects on the risk of developing diseases. Harmful variants in some genes are known to be associated with an increased risk of developing cancer. These inherited variants are thought to contribute to about 5 to 10% of all cancers.</p>

945

946

947<p>

948Cancer can sometimes appear to “<span>

949<a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000256552&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000256552&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

950run in families</a>

951</span>

952” even if it is not caused by an inherited variant. For example, a shared environment or lifestyle, such as tobacco use, can cause similar cancers to develop among family members. However, certain patterns that are seen in members of a family—such as the types of cancer that develop, other non-cancer conditions that are seen, and the ages at which cancer typically develops—may suggest the presence of an inherited susceptibility to cancer.</p>

953

954

955<p>

956Genes involved in many of the known <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000754220&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000754220&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

957inherited cancer susceptibility syndromes</a>

958 have been identified. Testing whether someone carries a harmful variant in one of these genes can confirm whether a condition is, indeed, the result of an inherited syndrome. Genetic testing is also done to determine whether family members who have not (yet) developed a cancer have inherited the same variant as a family member who is known to carry a harmful (cancer susceptibility predisposing) variant.</p>

959

960

961<p>

962A different type of genetic testing, called <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000768570&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000768570&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

963tumor DNA sequencing</a>

964, is sometimes done to determine if cancer cells of people who have already gotten a cancer diagnosis have genetic changes that can be used to guide treatment. Although some of these cancer cell changes may be inherited, most occur randomly during a person’s lifetime. Genetic testing of tumor cells is addressed in the <span>

965<a data-entity-substitution="canonical" data-entity-type="node" data-entity-uuid="97df430a-402e-4239-aa18-4ed819cbfa37" href="/about-cancer/treatment/types/biomarker-testing-cancer-treatment">

966Biomarker Testing for Cancer Treatment</a>

967 </span>

968page.</p>

980 <h2 id="does-someone-who-inherits-a-cancer-susceptibility-variant-always-get-cancer" itemprop="name">

981

982 <p>

983Does someone who inherits a cancer susceptibility variant always get cancer?</p>

984

985</h2>

986

987 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

988

989 <div itemprop="text">

990

991<p>

992No. Even if a <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000794887&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000794887&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

993cancer susceptibility variant</a>

994 is present in a family, it does not necessarily mean that everyone who inherits the variant will develop cancer. Several factors influence whether a given person with the variant will actually develop cancer. One is the <strong>

995<a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000339344&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000339344&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

996penetrance</a>

997</strong>

998 of the variant. When not all people who carry a variant go on to develop the disease associated with that variant, it is said to have incomplete or reduced penetrance. Hereditary cancer syndromes can also vary in their <strong>

999expressivity</strong>

1000—that is, people who inherit the variant may vary in the extent to which they show signs and symptoms of the syndrome, including the development of associated cancers. Lifestyle factors and environmental risks can also influence disease expression.</p>

1014 <p>

1015What genetic tests are available for cancer risk assessment?</p>

1016

1017</h2>

1018

1019 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1020

1021 <div itemprop="text">

1022

1023<p>

1024More than 50 <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000754220&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000754220&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1025hereditary cancer syndromes</a>

1026 have been described; see the PDQ Cancer Genetics Overview for <span>

1027<a href="https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/causes-prevention/genetics/overview-pdq#_123">

1028a list of familial cancer susceptibility syndromes</a>

1029</span>

1030. Most of these are caused by harmful variants that are inherited in an <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000793860&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000793860&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1031autosomal dominant fashion</a>

1032—that is, a single altered copy of the gene inherited from one parent is enough to increase a person’s chance of developing cancer. For most of these syndromes, genetic tests for harmful variants are available.</p>

1033

1034

1035<p>

1036Tests are also available for several inherited genetic variants that are not associated with named syndromes but have been found to increase cancer risk. Examples include inherited variants in <em>

1037PALB2</em>

1038 (associated with increased risks of breast and pancreatic cancers), <em>

1039CHEK2</em>

1040 (breast and colorectal cancers), <em>

1041BRIP1</em>

1042 (ovarian cancer), and <em>

1043RAD51C</em>

1044 and <em>

1045RAD51D</em>

1046 (ovarian cancer).</p>

1060 <p>

1061Who should consider genetic testing for cancer risk?</p>

1062

1063</h2>

1064

1065 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1066

1067 <div itemprop="text">

1068

1069<p>

1070People who are concerned about whether their <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000302456&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000302456&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1071family history</a>

1072 puts them at risk for cancer should consult with a <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000794108&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000794108&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1073genetic counselor</a>

1074.</p>

1075

1076

1077<p>

1263

1264</h2>

1265

1266 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1267

1268 <div itemprop="text">

1269

1270<p>

1271Genetic testing can give several possible results: positive, negative, true negative, uninformative negative, <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000556495&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000556495&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1293

1294</ul>

1301Being checked at a younger age or more often for signs of cancer</li>

1308

1309 <li>

1310Getting help to guide decisions about <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046399&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000046399&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1320 A <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000044692&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000044692&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1340.</p>

1428

1429</h2>

1430

1431 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1432

1433 <div itemprop="text">

1434

1435<p>

1436An increasing number of companies offer at-home genetic testing, also known as direct-to-consumer (DTC) genetic testing. People collect a <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000257526&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000257526&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1437saliva</a>

1438 sample or a mouth swab themselves and submit the sample through the mail. They learn about the test results on a secure website, by mail, or over the phone.</p>

1439

1440

1441<p>

1442The genetic testing for cancer risk that is typically ordered by a doctor involves testing for inherited genetic variants that are associated with a high to moderate increased risk of cancer and are responsible for inherited cancer susceptibility syndromes. By contrast, DTC genetic testing for cancer risk often involves the analysis of common inherited genetic variants that, individually, are generally associated with only a minor increase in risk. Even when added together, all the known common variants associated with a particular cancer type account for only a small portion of a person’s risk of that cancer. Genetic tests based on these common variants have not yet been found to help patients and their care providers make health care decisions and, therefore, they are not a part of recommended clinical practice. </p>

1443

1444

1445<p>

1446Even when people have DTC genetic tests for gene variants that are known to be associated with inherited cancer susceptibility syndromes, there are potential risks and drawbacks to the use of DTC testing. For instance, some DTC genetic tests look for variants in the <em>

1447<a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046047&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000046047&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1448BRCA1</a>

1449</em>

1450 and <em>

1451<a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046742&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000046742&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1452BRCA2</a>

1453</em>

1454 genes that are associated with <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000751862&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000751862&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1455Hereditary Breast and Ovarian Cancer Syndrome</a>

1456 (HBOC). However, this testing looks only for three specific variants out of the thousands that have been identified. Therefore, someone could have a negative result with this kind of test but still have a harmful <em>

1457BRCA1</em>

1458 or <em>

1459BRCA2</em>

1460 gene variant that was just not identified by that test. In particular, without guidance about the most appropriate genetic testing to do and interpretation of the genetic test results from a knowledgeable health care provider, people may experience unneeded anxiety or false reassurance, or they may make important decisions about medical treatment or care based on incomplete information.</p>

1464DTC genetic testing also does not ensure the privacy of the test results. Companies’ disclosure policies are not always provided, or they may be difficult to find and understand. In addition, companies that provide DTC testing may not be subject to current state and federal privacy laws and regulations. It is generally recommended that people considering DTC genetic testing make sure that they have chosen a reputable company (i.e., one that fully and clearly discloses its privacy policy).</p>

1494 <p>

1495What are some of the benefits of genetic testing for inherited cancer susceptibility syndromes?</p>

1496

1497</h2>

1498

1499 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1500

1501 <div itemprop="text">

1502

1503<p>

1504There can be benefits to genetic testing, regardless of whether a person receives a positive or a negative result.</p>

1505

1506

1507<ul>

1508<li>

1509An informative negative test can provide the person with peace of mind that a harmful gene variant was not inherited.</li>

1510

1511 <li>

1512A positive test result provides the person an opportunity to understand and, in some cases, manage their cancer risks.</li>

1513

1514 <li>

1515For people who are already diagnosed with a cancer, results of genetic testing may help them make decisions about their treatment and understand their risk for other cancers.</li>

1516

1517 <li>

1518Genetic testing provides an opportunity for family members to learn about their own cancer risks.</li>

1519

1520</ul>

1533 <p>

1534What are some of the possible harms of genetic testing for inherited cancer susceptibility syndromes?</p>

1535

1536</h2>

1537

1538 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1539

1540 <div itemprop="text">

1541

1542<p>

1543Genetic testing can have potential emotional, social, and financial harms, including:</p>

1544

1545

1546<ul>

1547<li>

1548Psychological stress of learning that one has a genetic variant that increases cancer risk and having to decide whether to share those findings with blood relatives</li>

1549

1550 <li>

1551An uninformative test results, such as a report of a <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000556495&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000556495&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1552variant of uncertain significance</a>

1553 (VUS), increases uncertainty and may increase stress until results are clarified</li>

1554

1555 <li>

1556Survivor guilt upon learning that one doesn’t have a harmful variant that is present on other members of the family</li>

1557

1558 <li>

1559Cost of testing itself and additional follow-up testing, if not covered by insurance </li>

1560

1561 <li>

1562Privacy and discrimination issues</li>

1563

1564 <li>

1565Incorrect or misleading information provided by DTC or clinical genetic testing</li>

1566

1567</ul>

1578 <h2 id="how-are-genetic-tests-regulated" itemprop="name">

1579

1580 <p>

1581How are genetic tests regulated?</p>

1582

1583</h2>

1584

1585 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1586

1587 <div itemprop="text">

1588

1589<p>

1590U.S. laboratories that perform health-related testing, including genetic testing, are regulated under the Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA) program. Laboratories that are certified under CLIA are required to meet federal standards for quality, accuracy, and reliability of tests. All laboratories that do genetic testing and share results must be CLIA certified. However, CLIA certification only indicates that appropriate laboratory quality control standards are being followed; it does not guarantee that a genetic test being done by a laboratory is medically useful or properly interpreted. The National Human Genome Research Institute has more information available on its <span>

1591<a href="https://www.genome.gov/10002335/regulation-of-genetic-tests/">

1592Regulation of Genetics Tests</a>

1593</span>

1594 page.</p>

1606 <h2 id="what-research-is-being-done-to-improve-genetic-testing-for-cancer" itemprop="name">

1607

1608 <p>

1609What research is being done to improve genetic testing for cancer?</p>

1610

1611</h2>

1612

1613 <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">

1614

1615 <div itemprop="text">

1616

1617<p>

1618Research is ongoing to find better ways to detect, treat, and prevent cancer in people who carry genetic variants that increase the risk of certain cancers. Scientists are also doing studies to find additional genetic changes that can increase a person’s risk of cancer. There is also much work being done to increase the accuracy and consistency of classifying the genetic variants detected by testing.</p>

1619

1620

1621<p>

1622The goal of another avenue of research is to provide doctors and patients with better information about the cancer risks associated with specific genetic variants, particularly variants of uncertain significance. For example, one research approach, called saturation genome editing, used <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000787460&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000787460&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1623CRISPR-Cas9</a>

1624 gene editing to create 4000 different genetic variants throughout a region of the <em>

1625BRCA1</em>

1626 gene that is important for its function as a <a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000046657&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000046657&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1627tumor suppressor</a>

1628. The gene editing was done in special cells that cannot survive without a functioning BRCA1 protein. For variants where clinical data were available, the gene editing results agreed with clinical findings more than 96% of the time, suggesting that this approach can be used to classify the cancer risks associated with variants of uncertain significance in other genes.</p>

1629

1630

1631<p>

1632Collaborative efforts that bring together genomic and clinical data, such as the <span>

1633<a href="https://brcaexchange.org/">

1634BRCA Exchange</a>

1635</span>

1636 web portal, are providing a comprehensive resource that people can refer to when they get the results of a genetic test and want to know the clinical significance of particular gene variant. <span>

1637<a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/clinvar/">

1638ClinVar</a>

1639</span>

1640 is a public archive to support the accumulation of evidence for the clinical significance of genetic variants. </p>

1641

1642

1643<p>

1644NCI runs an active program of <span>

1645<a href="https://dceg.cancer.gov/research/how-we-study/genomic-studies/gwas-overview">

1646genome-wide association studies</a>

1647</span>

1648 (<a class="definition" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=CDR0000636780&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000636780&amp;version=Patient&amp;language=English'); return false;">

1649GWAS</a>

1650) through its <span>

1651<a href="https://dceg.cancer.gov/about/organization/programs-hgp/cgr">

1652Cancer Genomics Research Laboratory</a>

1653</span>

1654. This technique compares the genomes from many different people to find genetic markers associated with particular observable characteristics or risk of disease. The goal is to understand how genes contribute to the disease and to use that understanding to help develop better prevention and treatment strategies. </p>

1655

1656

1657<p>

1658Additional NCI research is focused on improving genetic counseling methods and outcomes, studying the risks and benefits of at-home genetic testing, and evaluating the effects of advertising of these tests on patients, providers, and the health care system. Researchers are also working to improve the laboratory methods available for genetic testing.</p>
