919<section id="_7">

920<h3 id="_7_toc">

921Incidence and Mortality</h3>

922<p id="_16" tabindex="-1">

923Estimated new cases and deaths from pancreatic cancer in the United States in 2023:[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9241</a>

925]</p>

926<div class="pdq-content-list">

927<ul id="_17">

928<li>

929New cases: 64,050.</li>

930<li>

931Deaths: 50,550.</li>

932</ul>

933</div>

934<p id="_18" tabindex="-1">

935The incidence of pancreatic cancer has markedly increased over the past several decades. It ranks as the fourth leading cause of cancer death in the United States.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9361</a>

937] Despite the high mortality rate associated with pancreatic

938cancer, its etiology is poorly understood. </p>

939</section>

940<section id="_1957">

941<h3 id="_1957_toc">

942Risk Factors</h3>

943<p id="_1958" tabindex="-1">

944Risk factors for development of pancreatic cancer include the following:[<a href="#cit/section_1.2">

9452</a>

946,<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

9473</a>

948]</p>

949<div class="pdq-content-list">

950<ul id="_1959">

951<li>

952A family history of pancreatic cancer.</li>

953<li>

954Cigarette smoking.</li>

955<li>

956Obesity.</li>

957<li>

958Chronic pancreatitis.</li>

959<li>

960Certain genetic disorders (such as those associated with the <em class="gene-name">

961BRCA1</em>

962, <em class="gene-name">

963BRCA2</em>

964, <em class="gene-name">

965PALB2</em>

966, and <em class="gene-name">

967ATM</em>

968 genes).</li>

969</ul>

970</div>

971</section>

972<section id="_22">

973<h3 id="_22_toc">

974Anatomy</h3>

975<p id="_24" tabindex="-1">

976<figure id="figure_181" class="image-center-medium">

977<a href="https://nci-media.cancer.gov/pdq/media/images/636528.jpg" target="_blank" class="article-image-enlarge">

978Enlarge</a>

979<img id="_181" alt="Pancreas" title="Pancreas" src="https://nci-media.cancer.gov/pdq/media/images/636528-750.jpg">

980<figcaption class="caption-container">

981Anatomy of the pancreas.</figcaption>

982</figure>

983</p>

984<p id="_1901" tabindex="-1">

985Cancers of the pancreas are commonly identified by the site of involvement within the pancreas. Surgical approaches differ for masses in the head, body, tail, or uncinate process of the pancreas.</p>

986</section>

987<section id="_29">

988<h3 id="_29_toc">

989Clinical Features</h3>

990<p id="_31" tabindex="-1">

991Pancreatic cancer symptoms depend on the site of the tumor within the pancreas and the degree of tumor involvement. </p>

992<p id="_1884" tabindex="-1">

993In the early stages of pancreatic cancer, there are not many noticeable symptoms. As the cancer grows, symptoms may include the following:</p>

994<div class="pdq-content-list">

995<ul id="_52">

996<li>

997Jaundice.</li>

998<li>

999Light-colored stools or dark urine.</li>

1000<li>

1001Pain in the upper or middle abdomen and back.</li>

1002<li>

1003Weight loss for no known reason.</li>

1004<li>

1005Loss of appetite.</li>

1006<li>

1007Fatigue.</li>

1008</ul>

1009</div>

1010</section>

1011<section id="_38">

1012<h3 id="_38_toc">

1013Diagnostic and Staging Evaluation</h3>

1014<p id="_1902" tabindex="-1">

1015Pancreatic cancer is difficult to detect and diagnose for the following reasons:</p>

1016<div class="pdq-content-list">

1017<ul id="_1903">

1018<li>

1019There are no noticeable signs or symptoms in the early stages of pancreatic cancer.</li>

1020<li>

1021The signs of pancreatic cancer, when present, are like the signs of many other illnesses, such as pancreatitis or an ulcer.</li>

1022<li>

1023The pancreas is obscured by other organs in the abdomen and is difficult to visualize clearly on imaging tests.</li>

1024</ul>

1025</div>

1026<p id="_1904" tabindex="-1">

1027To appropriately treat pancreatic cancer, it is crucial to evaluate whether the cancer can be resected. </p>

1028<section id="_43">

1029<h4 id="_43_toc">

1030Imaging</h4>

1031<p id="_192" tabindex="-1">

1032The use of imaging technology may aid in the diagnosis of pancreatic cancer and in the identification of patients with

1033disease that is not amenable to resection. Imaging tests may include the following:[<a href="#cit/section_1.4">

10344</a>

1035]</p>

1036<div class="pdq-content-list">

1037<ul id="_59">

1038<li>

1039Helical computed tomographic scan.</li>

1040<li>

1041Magnetic resonance imaging scan.</li>

1042<li>

1043Endoscopic ultrasonography.</li>

1044<li>

1045Minimally invasive techniques, such as laparoscopy and laparoscopic ultrasonography may be used to decrease the use of laparotomy.[<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

10465</a>

1047,<a href="#cit/section_1.6">

10486</a>

1049]</li>

1050</ul>

1051</div>

1052</section>

1053<section id="_45">

1054<h4 id="_45_toc">

1055Peritoneal cytology</h4>

1056<p id="_46" tabindex="-1">

1057In a case series of 228 patients, positive peritoneal cytology had a positive predictive value of 94%, specificity of 98%, and sensitivity of 25% for determining unresectability.[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

10587</a>

1059]</p>

1060</section>

1061<section id="_47">

1062<h4 id="_47_toc">

1063Tumor markers</h4>

1064<p id="_48" tabindex="-1">

1065No tumor-specific markers exist for pancreatic cancer; markers such as serum cancer antigen (CA) 19-9 have low specificity. Most patients with pancreatic cancer have an elevated CA 19-9 level at diagnosis. Following or during definitive therapy, the increase of CA 19-9 levels may identify patients with progressive tumor growth.[<a href="#cit/section_1.8">

10668</a>

1067][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1068Level of evidence C2</a>

1069] The presence of a normal CA 19-9, however, does not preclude recurrence.</p>

1070</section>

1071</section>

1072<section id="_49">

1073<h3 id="_49_toc">

1074Prognosis and Survival</h3>

1075<p id="_1905" tabindex="-1">

1076The primary factors that influence prognosis are:</p>

1077<div class="pdq-content-list">

1078<ul id="_1906">

1079<li>

1080Whether the tumor is localized and can be completely resected.</li>

1081<li>

1082Whether the tumor has spread to lymph nodes or elsewhere.</li>

1083</ul>

1084</div>

1085<p id="_1908" tabindex="-1">

1086Exocrine pancreatic cancer is rarely curable and has an overall survival (OS) rate of less than 6%.[<a href="#cit/section_1.9">

10879</a>

1088] As pancreatic cancer is associated with significant morbidity and mortality, and treatment decisions are complex, management with a comprehensive multidisciplinary team should be considered.</p>

1089<p id="_1909" tabindex="-1">

1090 The highest cure rate occurs when the tumor is truly localized to the pancreas; however, this stage of disease accounts for less than 20% of cases. For patients with localized disease and small cancers (&lt;2 cm) with no lymph node metastases and no extension beyond the capsule of the pancreas, complete surgical resection is associated with an actuarial 5-year survival rate of 18% to 24%.[<a href="#cit/section_1.10">

109110</a>

1092][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1093Level of evidence C1</a>

1094]</p>

1095<p id="_1910" tabindex="-1">

1096Surgical resection is the mainstay of curative treatment and provides a survival benefit in patients with small, localized pancreatic tumors, but should be considered only alongside systemic therapy. Patients with unresectable, metastatic, or recurrent disease are unlikely to benefit from surgical resection.</p>

1097<p id="_1911" tabindex="-1">

1098Patients with any stage of pancreatic cancer can be candidates for clinical trials because of the poor response to chemotherapy,

1099radiation therapy, and surgery as conventionally used.</p>

1100<p id="_2130" tabindex="-1">

1101Information about ongoing clinical trials for pancreatic cancer is available from the <a href="https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/clinical-trials" title="https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/clinical-trials">

1102NCI website</a>

1103.</p>

1104</section>

1105<section id="_70">

1106<h3 id="_70_toc">

1107Palliative Therapy</h3>

1108<p id="_72" tabindex="-1">

1109Palliation of symptoms may be achieved with conventional treatment (systematic chemotherapy).</p>

1110<p id="_73" tabindex="-1">

1111Palliative measures that may improve quality of life while not affecting OS include the following:[<a href="#cit/section_1.11">

111211</a>

1113,<a href="#cit/section_1.12">

111412</a>

1115]</p>

1116<div class="pdq-content-list">

1117<ul id="_74">

1118<li>

1119Surgical or radiological biliary decompression.</li>

1293<p id="_91" tabindex="-1">

1294The staging system for pancreatic exocrine cancer continues to evolve. Clinical staging is guided by resectability, which is strongly influenced by surgical judgment. Consensus guidelines for surgical resectability (e.g., National Comprehensive Cancer Network, MD Anderson Cancer Center, American Hepato-Pancreato-Biliary Association, and International Hepato-Pancreato-Biliary Association) continue to be refined, but are traditionally stratified by the following:</p>

1295<div class="pdq-content-list">

1296<ul id="_2051">

1297<li>

1298 Resectable: tumors without vascular involvement.</li>

1299<li>

1300 Borderline resectable: tumors with involvement of vasculature, involvement of local structures, or other evidence of a high risk of R1 resection.</li>

1301<li>

1302 Locally advanced: tumors with local invasion (primarily vascular involvement) that preclude surgical intervention.</li>

1303<li>

1304 Metastatic: cancer that has spread beyond the primary pancreatic tumor to other organs.</li>

1305</ul>

1306</div>
