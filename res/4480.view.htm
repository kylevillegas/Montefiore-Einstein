910<p id="_20" tabindex="-1">

911Pheochromocytomas and extra-adrenal paragangliomas are rare tumors arising from neural crest tissue that develops into sympathetic and parasympathetic paraganglia throughout the body.</p>

912<p id="_24" tabindex="-1">

913In 2004, the World Health Organization classification utilized the term <em>

914pheochromocytoma</em>

915 exclusively for tumors arising from the adrenal medulla, and the term <em>

916extra-adrenal paraganglioma</em>

917 for similar tumors that arise from other locations.</p>

918<section id="_25">

919<h3 id="_25_toc">

920Incidence and Mortality</h3>

921<p id="_160" tabindex="-1">

922The incidence of pheochromocytoma is 2 to 8 per million persons per year.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9231</a>

924,<a href="#cit/section_1.2">

9252</a>

926] Pheochromocytoma is present in 0.1% to 1% of patients with hypertension,[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

9273</a>

928-<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

9295</a>

930] and it is present in approximately 5% of patients with incidentally discovered adrenal masses.[<a href="#cit/section_1.6">

9316</a>

932] The peak incidence occurs in the third to fifth decades of life; the average age at diagnosis is 24.9 years in hereditary cases and 43.9 years in sporadic cases.[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

9337</a>

934] The incidence is equal between men and women.[<a href="#cit/section_1.8">

9358</a>

936]</p>

937</section>

938<section id="_27">

939<h3 id="_27_toc">

940Anatomy</h3>

941<p id="_72" tabindex="-1">

942Pheochromocytomas and extra-adrenal paragangliomas arise from neural crest tissue. Neural crest tissue develops into sympathetic and parasympathetic paraganglia.</p>

943<p id="_157" tabindex="-1">

944Sympathetic paraganglia include the following:</p>

945<div class="pdq-content-list">

946<ul id="_21">

947<li>

948The adrenal medulla.</li>

949<li>

950The organ of Zuckerkandl near the aortic bifurcation.</li>

951<li>

952Other paraganglia along the distribution of the sympathetic nervous system.</li>

953</ul>

954</div>

955<p id="_22" tabindex="-1">

956Parasympathetic paraganglia include the following:</p>

957<div class="pdq-content-list">

958<ul id="_23">

959<li>

960The carotid body.</li>

961<li>

962Other paraganglia along the cervical and thoracic branches of the vagus and glossopharyngeal nerves.</li>

963</ul>

964</div>

965</section>

966<section id="_194">

967<h3 id="_194_toc">

968Risk Factors</h3>

969<p id="_161" tabindex="-1">

970No known environmental, dietary, or lifestyle risk factors have been linked to the development of pheochromocytoma.</p>

971</section>

1146<h4 id="_159_toc">

1147Genetic counseling and testing</h4>

1148<p id="_75" tabindex="-1">

1149It has been proposed that all patients diagnosed with a pheochromocytoma or paraganglioma should consider genetic testing because the incidence of a hereditary syndrome in apparently sporadic cases is as high as 25%.[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

11507</a>

1151,<a href="#cit/section_1.8">

11528</a>

1153,<a href="#cit/section_1.23">

115423</a>

1155] Early identification of a hereditary syndrome allows for early screening for other associated tumors and identification of family members who are at risk. In addition, some patients with a hereditary syndrome are more likely to develop multifocal, malignant, or recurrent disease. Knowledge of the specific genetic mutation permits increased vigilance during preoperative localization or postoperative surveillance of such patients.</p>

1156<p id="_76" tabindex="-1">

1157Certain subgroups of patients are at very low risk of having an inherited syndrome (e.g., &lt;2% in patients diagnosed with apparently sporadic pheochromocytoma after age 50 years).[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

11587</a>

1159] Therefore, genetic testing for all patients diagnosed with a pheochromocytoma or paraganglioma may not be practical or cost effective from a population standpoint. It is recommended that every patient diagnosed with a pheochromocytoma or extra-adrenal paraganglioma should first undergo risk evaluation for a hereditary syndrome by a certified genetic counselor.[<a href="#cit/section_1.24">

116024</a>

1161] </p>

1162<p id="_77" tabindex="-1">

1163Genetic testing is often recommended in the following situations:</p>

1164<div class="pdq-content-list">

1165<ul id="_78">

1166<li>

1167Patients with a personal or family history of clinical features suggestive of a hereditary pheochromocytoma-paraganglioma syndrome.</li>

1168<li>

1169Patients with bilateral or multifocal tumors.</li>

1170<li>

1171Patients with sympathetic or malignant extra-adrenal paragangliomas.</li>

1172<li>

1173Patients diagnosed before age 40 years.</li>

1174</ul>

1175</div>

1176<p id="_79" tabindex="-1">

1177In patients with a unilateral pheochromocytoma and no personal or family history suggestive of hereditary disease, genetic testing can be considered if patients are between the ages of 40 years and 50 years, but genetic testing is generally not recommended if patients are older than 50 years. If a mutation is identified, predictive genetic testing should be offered to asymptomatic at-risk family members. For more information, see <a href="/types/thyroid/hp/medullary-thyroid-genetics-pdq">

1178Genetics of Endocrine and Neuroendocrine Neoplasias</a>

1179.</p>

1183<h3 id="_38_toc">

1184Clinical Features</h3>

1185<p id="_39" tabindex="-1">

1186Patients with pheochromocytomas and sympathetic extra-adrenal paragangliomas may present with symptoms of excess catecholamine production, including the following:</p>

1187<div class="pdq-content-list">

1188<ul id="_40">

1189<li>

1190Hypertension.</li>

1191<li>

1192Headache.</li>

1193<li>

1194Perspiration.</li>

1195<li>

1196Forceful palpitations.</li>

1197<li>

1198Tremor.</li>

1199<li>

1200Facial pallor.</li>

1201</ul>

1202</div>

1203<p id="_41" tabindex="-1">

1204These symptoms are often paroxysmal, although sustained hypertension between paroxysmal episodes occurs in 50% to 60% of patients with pheochromocytoma.[<a href="#cit/section_1.25">

120525</a>

1206] Episodes of hypertension can be variable in frequency, severity, and duration and are often extremely difficult to manage medically. Hypertensive crisis can lead to cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial infarction, and even death.</p>

1207<p id="_162" tabindex="-1">

1208Patients are often very symptomatic from excess catecholamine secretion. Symptoms of catecholamine excess can be spontaneous or induced by the following: </p>

1209<div class="pdq-content-list">

1210<ul id="_42">

1211<li>

1212Strenuous physical exertion.</li>

1213<li>

1214Trauma.</li>

1215<li>

1216Labor and delivery.</li>

1217<li>

1218Anesthesia induction.</li>

1219<li>

1220Surgery or other invasive procedures, including direct instrumentation of the tumor (e.g., fine-needle aspiration).</li>

1221<li>

1222Eating foods high in tyramine (e.g., red wine, chocolate, and cheese).</li>

1223<li>

1224Urination (e.g., bladder wall tumor, which is rare).</li>

1225</ul>

1226</div>

1227<p id="_163" tabindex="-1">

1228Phenoxybenzamine (an alpha-adrenergic receptor blocker) is an effective treatment for catecholamine excess and metyrosine (a catecholamine synthesis antagonist) can be added if needed.</p>

1229<p id="_44" tabindex="-1">

1230Parasympathetic extra-adrenal paragangliomas do not secrete catecholamines. These tumors usually present as a neck mass with symptoms related to compression or are incidentally discovered on an imaging study performed for an unrelated reason. In addition, approximately half of patients with pheochromocytoma are asymptomatic because their neoplasms are discovered in the presymptomatic state by either abdominal imaging for other reasons (e.g., adrenal incidentalomas) or genetic testing in at-risk family members.[<a href="#cit/section_1.20">

123120</a>

1232,<a href="#cit/section_1.26">

123326</a>

1234-<a href="#cit/section_1.28">

123528</a>

1236]</p>

1238<section id="_45">

1239<h3 id="_45_toc">

1240Diagnostics</h3>

1241<p id="_164" tabindex="-1">

1242The diagnosis of pheochromocytoma is usually suspected by the presence of an adrenal mass or is discovered incidentally. Biochemical testing is done to document excess catecholamine secretion. Once the biochemical diagnosis of a catecholamine-secreting tumor is confirmed, localization studies should be performed. Controversy exists as to the optimal single test to make the diagnosis.</p>

1243<section id="_165">

1244<h4 id="_165_toc">

1245Biochemical testing</h4>

1246<section id="_90">

1247<h5 id="_90_toc">

124824-hour urine collection</h5>

1249<p id="_91" tabindex="-1">

1250A 24-hour urine collection for catecholamines (e.g., epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine) and fractionated metanephrines (e.g., metanephrine and normetanephrine) has a relatively low sensitivity (77%&#8211;90%) but a high specificity (98%). Pretest probability is also important. The specificity of plasma-free fractionated metanephrines is 82% in patients tested for sporadic pheochromocytoma versus 96% in patients tested for hereditary pheochromocytoma.[<a href="#cit/section_1.29">

125129</a>

1252,<a href="#cit/section_1.30">

125330</a>

1254]</p>

1255</section>

1256<section id="_92">

1257<h5 id="_92_toc">

1258Plasma-free fractionated metanephrines</h5>

1259<p id="_93" tabindex="-1">

1260Measurement of plasma-free fractionated metanephrines appears to be an ideal case-detection test for patients at higher baseline risk of pheochromocytoma. Examples of these patients might include the following:</p>

1261<div class="pdq-content-list">

1262<ul id="_49">

1263<li>

1264Patients with an incidentally discovered adrenal mass.</li>

1265<li>

1266Patients with a family history of pheochromocytoma.</li>

1267<li>

1268Patients with a known inherited predisposition to pheochromocytoma.</li>

1269</ul>

1270</div>

1271<p id="_50" tabindex="-1">

1272The test is associated with a relatively high false-positive rate in patients with a lower baseline risk of pheochromocytoma. Measurement of plasma-free metanephrines (e.g., metanephrine and normetanephrine) has a high sensitivity (97%&#8211;99%) but a relatively low specificity (85%).</p>

1273<p id="_51" tabindex="-1">

1274In general, it is reasonable to use measurement of plasma-free fractionated metanephrines for initial case detection, which is followed by 24-hour measurement of urine-fractionated metanephrines and catecholamines for confirmation. Test results can be difficult to interpret because of the possibility of false-positive results. False-positive results can be caused by any of the following:[<a href="#cit/section_1.25">

127525</a>

1276,<a href="#cit/section_1.29">

127729</a>

1278]</p>

1279<div class="pdq-content-list">

1280<ul id="_52">

1281<li>

1282Common medications (e.g., tricyclic antidepressants).</li>

1283<li>

1284Physical or emotional stress.</li>

1285<li>

1286Inappropriately low reference ranges based on normal laboratory data rather than clinical data sets.[<a href="#cit/section_1.31">

128731</a>

1288]</li>

1289<li>

1290Common foods (e.g., caffeine and bananas) that interfere with specific assays and medications.</li>

1291</ul>

1292</div>

1293<p id="_53" tabindex="-1">

1294A mildly elevated catecholamine or metanephrine level is usually the result of assay interference caused by drugs or other factors. Patients with symptomatic pheochromocytoma almost always have increases in catecholamines or metanephrines two to three times higher than the upper limits of reference ranges.[<a href="#cit/section_1.25">

129525</a>

1296] </p>

1297<p id="_166" tabindex="-1">

1298Provocative testing (e.g., using glucagon) can be dangerous, adds no value to other current testing methods, and is not recommended.[<a href="#cit/section_1.32">

129932</a>

1300]</p>

1303<section id="_94">

1304<h4 id="_94_toc">

1305Imaging studies</h4>

1306<p id="_81" tabindex="-1">

1307Computed tomography (CT) imaging or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the abdomen and pelvis (at least through the level of the aortic bifurcation) are the most commonly used methods for localization.[<a href="#cit/section_1.33">

130833</a>

1309] Both have similar sensitivities (90%&#8211;100%) and specificities (70%&#8211;80%).[<a href="#cit/section_1.33">

131033</a>

1311] CT imaging provides superior anatomic detail compared with MRI.</p>

1312<p id="_83" tabindex="-1">

1313Additional functional imaging may be necessary if CT imaging or MRI fails to localize the tumor. It might also be useful in patients who are at risk for multifocal, malignant, or recurrent disease. Iodine I 123 (123I)-metaiodobenzylguanidine (MIBG) scintigraphy coupled with CT imaging provides anatomic and functional information with good sensitivity (80%&#8211;90%) and specificity (95%&#8211;100%).[<a href="#cit/section_1.33">

131433</a>

1315] 131I-MIBG can be used in the same way, but the image quality is not as high as with 123I-MIBG.[<a href="#cit/section_1.34">

131634</a>

1317] Other functional imaging alternatives include gallium Ga 68 (68Ga)-DOTATATE and fluorine F 18-fludeoxyglucose positron emission tomography (PET), both of which can be coupled with CT imaging for improved anatomic detail.[<a href="#cit/section_1.35">

131835</a>

1319,<a href="#cit/section_1.36">

132036</a>

1321]</p>

1322<p id="_167" tabindex="-1">

1323It is rare for localization of a catecholamine-secreting tumor to be unsuccessful if currently available imaging methods are used.</p>

1327<h3 id="_64_toc">

1328Prognosis and Survival</h3>

1329<p id="_85" tabindex="-1">

1330There are no clear data regarding the survival of patients with localized (apparently benign) disease or regional disease. Although patients with localized (apparently benign) disease should experience an overall survival approaching that of age-matched disease-free individuals, 6.5% to 16.5% of these patients will develop a recurrence, usually 5 to 15 years after initial surgery.[<a href="#cit/section_1.37">

133137</a>

1332-<a href="#cit/section_1.39">

133339</a>

1334]</p>

1335<p id="_86" tabindex="-1">

1336Approximately 15% to 25% of patients with recurrent disease experience distant metastasis. The 5-year overall survival rates in those with metastatic disease range from 50% to 70%.[<a href="#cit/section_1.40">

133740</a>

1338-<a href="#cit/section_1.43">

133943</a>

1340] Carriers of <em class="gene-name">

1341SDHB</em>

1342 pathogenic variants have an increased risk of developing metastatic disease of approximately 25% to 50%.[<a href="#cit/section_1.44">

134344</a>

1344] The most commonly associated gene with metastatic pheochromocytoma and paraganglioma is <em class="gene-name">

1345SDHB</em>

1346 (over 40% of cases).[<a href="#cit/section_1.45">

134745</a>

1348,<a href="#cit/section_1.46">

134946</a>

1350]</p>

1353<h3 id="_71_toc">

1354Follow-up Evaluation</h3>

1355<p id="_168" tabindex="-1">

1356Long-term follow-up is essential for all patients with pheochromocytoma or extra-adrenal paraganglioma, even when initial pathology demonstrates no findings that are concerning for malignancy.[<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

13575</a>

1358]</p>

1359<div class="pdq-content-list">

1360<ul id="_169">

1361<li>

1362 After resection of a solitary sporadic pheochromocytoma, patients should undergo baseline postoperative biochemical testing followed by annual lifelong biochemical testing. </li>

1363<li>

1364Patients who have undergone resection of a noncatecholamine-producing tumor should initially undergo annual imaging with computed tomography imaging or magnetic resonance imaging and periodic imaging with radiolabeled metaiodobenzylguanidine or 68Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT to monitor for recurrence or metastasis.</li>

1365<li>

1366Patients who have undergone resection of a pheochromocytoma or paraganglioma in the setting of a hereditary syndrome require lifelong annual biochemical screening in addition to routine screening for other component tumors of their specific syndrome.[<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

13675</a>

1368]</li>

1583<h3 id="_95_toc">

1584Pathologic Classification</h3>

1585<p id="_96" tabindex="-1">

1586Pheochromocytoma and paraganglioma characteristically form small nests of uniform polygonal chromaffin cells (&#8220;zellballen&#8221;). A diagnosis of malignancy can only be made by identifying tumor deposits in tissues that do not normally contain chromaffin cells (e.g., lymph nodes, liver, bone, lung, and other distant metastatic sites). </p>

1587<p id="_97" tabindex="-1">

1588Regional or distant metastatic disease is documented on initial pathology in only 3% to 8% of patients; thus, an attempt has been made to identify tumor characteristics associated with future malignant behavior. Pathologic features associated with malignancy include the following:

1589

1590</p>

1591<div class="pdq-content-list">

1592<ul id="_98">

1593<li>

1594Large tumor size.</li>

1595<li>

1596Increased number of mitoses.</li>

1597<li>

1598DNA aneuploidy.</li>

1599<li>

1600Extensive tumor necrosis. </li>

1601<li>

1602Vascular or capsular invasion.

1603</li>

1604</ul>

1605</div>

1606<p id="_99" tabindex="-1">

1607In the absence of clearly documented metastases, no combination of clinical, histopathologic, or biochemical features has been shown to reliably predict the biologic behavior of pheochromocytoma. If no definite malignancy is identified, pathology generally provides insufficient prognostic information regarding the likelihood of recurrence or metastasis. These tumors cannot be considered benign by default; patients require continued lifelong surveillance.[<a href="#cit/section_2.1">

2151<section id="_105">

2152<h3 id="_105_toc">

2153Preoperative Medical Preparation</h3>

2154<p id="_106" tabindex="-1">

2155Surgery is the mainstay of treatment for most patients; however, preoperative medical preparation is critical. Alpha-adrenergic blockade should be initiated at the time of diagnosis and maximized preoperatively to prevent potentially life-threatening cardiovascular complications, which can occur as a result of excess catecholamine secretion during surgery. Complications may include the following:</p>

2156<div class="pdq-content-list">

2157<ul id="_107">

2158<li>

2159Hypertensive crisis.</li>

2160<li>

2161Arrhythmia.</li>

2162<li>

2163Myocardial infarction.</li>

2164<li>

2165Pulmonary edema.</li>

2166</ul>

2167</div>

2168<p id="_108" tabindex="-1">

2169Phenoxybenzamine (a nonselective alpha-antagonist) is the usual drug of choice; prazosin, terazosin, and doxazosin (selective alpha-1-antagonists) are alternative choices.[<a href="#cit/section_4.1">

21701</a>

2171,<a href="#cit/section_4.2">

21722</a>

2173] Prazosin, terazosin, and doxazosin are shorter acting than phenoxybenzamine, and therefore, the duration of postoperative hypotension is theoretically less than with phenoxybenzamine; however, there is less overall experience with selective alpha-1-antagonists than with phenoxybenzamine.</p>

2174<p id="_109" tabindex="-1">

2175A preoperative treatment period of 1 to 3 weeks is usually sufficient; resolution of spells and a target low normal blood pressure for age indicate that alpha-adrenergic blockade is adequate. During alpha-adrenergic blockade, liberal salt and fluid intake should be encouraged because volume loading reduces excessive orthostatic hypotension both preoperatively and postoperatively. If tachycardia develops or if blood pressure control is not optimal with alpha-adrenergic blockade, a beta-adrenergic blocker (e.g., metoprolol or propranolol) can be added, but only after alpha-blockade. Beta-adrenergic blockade must never be initiated before alpha-adrenergic blockade; doing so blocks beta-adrenergic receptor-mediated vasodilation and results in unopposed alpha-adrenergic receptor-mediated vasoconstriction, which can lead to a life-threatening crisis.</p>

2245<h4 id="_297_toc">

2246Surgery</h4>

2247<p id="_227" tabindex="-1">

2248Surgical resection (i.e., adrenalectomy) is the definitive treatment for patients with localized pheochromocytoma. A minimally invasive adrenalectomy is generally the preferred approach if the following conditions can be met:</p>

2249<div class="pdq-content-list">

2250<ul id="_228">

2251<li>

2252Preoperative imaging reveals an adrenal pheochromocytoma that is approximately 6 cm or smaller in diameter.</li>

2253<li>

2254No radiographic evidence of invasion into adjacent structures or evidence of regional or metastatic disease (i.e., presumably a benign tumor).</li>

2255<li>

2256Normal contralateral adrenal gland.</li>

2257</ul>

2258</div>

2259<p id="_229" tabindex="-1">

2260Both anterior transabdominal laparoscopic adrenalectomy and posterior retroperitoneoscopic adrenalectomy have been demonstrated to be safe for most patients with a modestly sized, radiographically benign pheochromocytoma.[<a href="#cit/section_5.1">

22611</a>

2262,<a href="#cit/section_5.2">

22632</a>

2264] If preoperative imaging suggests malignancy, or if the patient has an extra-adrenal paraganglioma or multifocal disease, an open approach is generally preferred.</p>

2265<p id="_230" tabindex="-1">

2266Intraoperative hypertension can be controlled with intravenous infusion of phentolamine, sodium nitroprusside, or a short-acting calcium-channel blocker (e.g., nicardipine). Tumor removal may be followed by a sudden drop in blood pressure that may require rapid volume replacement and intravenous vasoconstrictors (e.g., norepinephrine or phenylephrine). Postoperatively, patients should remain in a monitored environment for 24 hours. Postoperative hypotension is managed primarily by volume expansion, and postoperative hypertension usually responds to diuretics.</p>

Bilateral total adrenalectomy commits all patients to lifelong steroid dependence, and up to 25% of patients will experience Addisonian crisis (acute adrenal insufficiency).[<a href="#cit/section_5.3">

2288,<a href="#cit/section_5.4">

22894</a>

2290]</p>

2292Recommendations generally favor preservation of adrenal cortical tissue in patients with MEN2 or VHL when possible. Patients who initially present with unilateral pheochromocytoma should undergo unilateral adrenalectomy, and patients who present with bilateral pheochromocytomas or who develop pheochromocytoma in their remaining adrenal gland should undergo cortical-sparing adrenalectomy, when technically feasible.[<a href="#cit/section_5.3">

22933</a>

2294]</p>

2475Treatment Options for Metastatic Pheochromocytoma</h3>

2476<p id="_250" tabindex="-1">

2477Treatment options for metastatic pheochromocytoma include the following:</p>

2478<div class="pdq-content-list">

2479<ol id="_252">

2480<li>

2481<a href="/types/pheochromocytoma/hp/pheochromocytoma-treatment-pdq#_254">

2482Surgery</a>

2483.</li>

2484<li>

2485<a href="/types/pheochromocytoma/hp/pheochromocytoma-treatment-pdq#_256">

2486Palliative therapy</a>

2487.</li>

2488</ol>

2489</div>

2490<p id="_253" tabindex="-1">

2491The most common sites of metastasis for pheochromocytoma or extra-adrenal paraganglioma are lymph nodes, bones, lungs, and liver. Patients with known or suspected malignancy should undergo staging with computed tomography or magnetic resonance imaging as well as functional imaging (e.g., with iodine I 123-metaiodobenzylguanidine [MIBG]) to determine the extent and location of disease. Patients are often very symptomatic from excess catecholamine secretion. Phenoxybenzamine is effective, and metyrosine, which is a drug that blocks catecholamine synthesis, can be added if needed.</p>

2492<section id="_254">

2493<h4 id="_254_toc">

2494Surgery</h4>

2495<p id="_255" tabindex="-1">

2496If all identifiable disease is resectable, including a limited number of distant metastases, surgery can provide occasional long-term remission. If disease is unresectable, surgical debulking will not improve survival; however, it is occasionally indicated for symptom palliation.</p>

2751Treatment Options for Recurrent Pheochromocytoma</h3>

2752<p id="_280" tabindex="-1">

2753Treatment options for recurrent pheochromocytoma include the following:</p>

2754<div class="pdq-content-list">

2766<p id="_283" tabindex="-1">

2767After resection of a localized pheochromocytoma presumed to represent a benign tumor and documented normal postoperative biochemical testing, disease recurrence occurs in 6.5% to 16.5% of patients, and 50% of patients with disease recurrence develop metastatic disease.[<a href="#cit/section_8.1">

27681</a>

2769-<a href="#cit/section_8.3">

27703</a>

2771] Insufficient data exist to determine recurrence rates after complete surgical resection of regional or metastatic disease.</p>

2772<section id="_284">

2773<h4 id="_284_toc">

2774Surgery</h4>

2775<p id="_285" tabindex="-1">

2776Treatment for recurrent disease involves appropriate medical management (i.e., alpha-adrenergic blockade) followed by complete surgical resection, when possible. </p>

2796Radiation therapy.</li>

2847 <h2>

2848Treatment of Pheochromocytoma During Pregnancy</h2>

2849

2850 <div id="_section_145" class="pdq-sections">

2851<nav class="in-this-section" role="navigation">

2852<h6>

2853In This Section</h6>

2854<ul>

2855<li>

2856<a href="#_191_toc">

2857Diagnosis</a>

2858</li>

2859<li>

2860<a href="#_192_toc">

2861Treatment Options for Pheochromocytoma During Pregnancy</a>

2862</li>

2863<li>

2864<a href="#_TrialSearch_145_sid_9_toc">

2865Current Clinical Trials</a>

2866</li>

2868</nav>

2869<p id="_146" tabindex="-1">

2870Pheochromocytoma diagnosed during pregnancy is extremely rare (0.007% of all pregnancies).[<a href="#cit/section_9.1">

28711</a>

2872,<a href="#cit/section_9.2">

28732</a>

2874] However, women with hereditary conditions that increase the risk of developing pheochromocytoma are often also of childbearing age, and the outcome of undiagnosed pheochromocytoma during pregnancy can be catastrophic.</p>

2875<section id="_191">

2876<h3 id="_191_toc">

2877Diagnosis</h3>

2878<p id="_147" tabindex="-1">

2879Prenatal diagnosis clearly results in decreased mortality for both mother and fetus.[<a href="#cit/section_9.3">

28803</a>

2881] Prior to 1970, a prenatal diagnosis of pheochromocytoma was made in only approximately 25% of cases, and the mortality rate for both mother and fetus was around 50%.[<a href="#cit/section_9.4">

28824</a>

2883,<a href="#cit/section_9.5">

28845</a>

2885] The prenatal diagnosis rate rose to greater than 80% through the 1980s and 1990s, and decreased maternal and fetal mortality rates were 6% and 15%, respectively.[<a href="#cit/section_9.4">

28864</a>

2887,<a href="#cit/section_9.6">

28886</a>

2889]</p>

2890<p id="_148" tabindex="-1">

2891The diagnosis of pheochromocytoma should be suspected in any pregnant woman who develops hypertension in the first trimester, paroxysmal hypertension, or hypertension that is unusually difficult to treat.[<a href="#cit/section_9.2">

28922</a>

2893,<a href="#cit/section_9.7">

28947</a>

2895] Normal pregnancy does not affect catecholamine levels.[<a href="#cit/section_9.8">

28968</a>

2897] Thus, the usual biochemical tests are valid. Magnetic resonance imaging is the localization method of choice because it does not expose the fetus to ionizing radiation.</p>

2898</section>

2899<section id="_192">

2900<h3 id="_192_toc">

2901Treatment Options for Pheochromocytoma During Pregnancy</h3>

2902<p id="_149" tabindex="-1">

2903Phenoxybenzamine use is safe in pregnancy, but beta-adrenergic blockers should be initiated only if needed because their use has been associated with intrauterine growth restriction.[<a href="#cit/section_9.9">

29049</a>

2905,<a href="#cit/section_9.10">

290610</a>

2907] Resection of the tumor can often be performed safely during the second trimester, or tumor resection can be combined with cesarean delivery for patients diagnosed later in pregnancy.[<a href="#cit/section_9.2">

29082</a>

2909] Case reports have documented successful outcomes in the rare circumstance when surgical resection was delayed until a short time after vaginal delivery.[<a href="#cit/section_9.11">

291011</a>

2911] The successful management of pheochromocytoma in pregnancy depends on careful monitoring and the availability of an experienced team of specialists.</p>
