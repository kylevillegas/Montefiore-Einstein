887 <h2>

888General Information About Bile Duct Cancer</h2>

927<p id="_83" tabindex="-1">

928Cancer of the bile duct (also called cholangiocarcinoma) is extremely rare. The true incidence of bile duct cancer is unknown because establishing an accurate diagnosis is difficult.</p>

929<p id="_226" tabindex="-1">

930Traditionally, bile duct tumors located within the liver were classified with hepatocellular carcinoma as primary liver tumors.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9311</a>

932] In contrast, bile duct tumors located outside of the liver were classified with gallbladder cancer as extrahepatic biliary tract tumors.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9331</a>

934] The classification of bile duct tumors has changed to include intrahepatic tumors of the bile ducts and extrahepatic tumors (perihilar and distal) of the bile ducts.</p>

935<p id="_341" tabindex="-1">

936Approximately 50% of cholangiocarcinomas arise in the bile ducts of the perihilar region, 40% in the distal region, and 10% in the intrahepatic region.</p>

937<p id="_3" tabindex="-1">

938Many bile duct cancers are multifocal. In most patients, the tumor cannot be completely removed by surgery and is incurable. Palliative measures such as resection, radiation therapy (e.g., brachytherapy or external-beam radiation therapy), or stenting procedures may maintain adequate biliary drainage and allow for improved quality of life. </p>

939<section id="_4">

940<h3 id="_4_toc">

941Anatomy</h3>

942<p id="_84" tabindex="-1">

943The biliary system consists of a network of ducts that carry bile from the liver to the small bowel and is classified by its anatomical location (Figure <a href="/types/liver/hp/bile-duct-treatment-pdq#_253">

9441</a>

945). Bile is produced by the liver and is important for fat digestion.</p>

946<section id="_349">

947<h4 id="_349_toc">

948Intrahepatic bile duct</h4>

949<p id="_309" tabindex="-1">

950The bile ducts located within the liver are called intrahepatic bile ducts. Tumors of the intrahepatic bile ducts originate in small intrahepatic ductules or large intrahepatic ducts that are proximal to the bifurcation of the right and left hepatic ducts. These tumors are also known as intrahepatic cholangiocarcinomas.</p>

952<a href="https://nci-media.cancer.gov/pdq/media/images/765897.jpg" target="_blank" class="article-image-enlarge">

959<section id="_159">

960<h4 id="_159_toc">

961Extrahepatic bile duct</h4>

962<p id="_229" tabindex="-1">

963The bile ducts located outside of the liver are called extrahepatic bile ducts. They include part of the right and left hepatic ducts that are outside of the liver, the common hepatic duct, and the common bile duct. The extrahepatic bile ducts can be further divided into the perihilar (hilum) region and distal region.</p>

964<figure id="figure_254" class="image-center-medium">

965<a href="https://nci-media.cancer.gov/pdq/media/images/659742.jpg" target="_blank" class="article-image-enlarge">

966Enlarge</a>

967<img id="_254" alt="Anatomy of the extrahepatic bile ducts; drawing shows the extrahepatic bile ducts, including the common hepatic duct (perihilar region) and the common bile duct (distal region). 968<figcaption class="caption-container">

969Figure 2. Anatomy of the extrahepatic bile duct.</figcaption>

970</figure>

971<div class="pdq-content-list">

972<ul id="_230">

973<li>

974<strong>

975Perihilar (hilum) region.</strong>

976 The hilum is the region where the right and left hepatic ducts exit the liver and join to form the common hepatic duct that is proximal to the origin of the cystic duct. Tumors of this region are also known as perihilar cholangiocarcinomas or Klatskin tumors.</li>

977<li>

978<strong>

979Distal region.</strong>

980 This region includes the common bile duct and inserts into the small intestine. Tumors of this region are also known as extrahepatic cholangiocarcinomas (Figure <a href="/types/liver/hp/bile-duct-treatment-pdq#_254">

9812</a>

982).</li>

983</ul>

984</div>

987<section id="_91">

988<h3 id="_91_toc">

989Risk Factors</h3>

990<p id="_92" tabindex="-1">

991Bile duct cancer may occur more frequently in patients with

992a history of primary sclerosing cholangitis, chronic ulcerative colitis,

993choledochal cysts, or infections with the liver fluke <em>

994Clonorchis sinensis</em>

995.[<a href="#cit/section_1.2">

9962</a>

997] </p>

998</section>

999<section id="_231">

1000<h3 id="_231_toc">

1001Clinical Features</h3>

1002<p id="_232" tabindex="-1">

1003Distal and perihilar bile duct cancers frequently cause biliary tract obstruction, leading to the following symptoms: </p>

1004<div class="pdq-content-list">

1005<ul id="_233">

1006<li>

1007 Jaundice.</li>

1008<li>

1009Weight loss.</li>

1010<li>

1011Abdominal pain.</li>

1012<li>

1013Fever.</li>

1014<li>

1015Pruritus.</li>

1016</ul>

1017</div>

1018<p id="_234" tabindex="-1">

1019Intrahepatic bile duct cancer may be relatively indolent and difficult to differentiate clinically from metastatic adenocarcinoma deposits in the liver.</p>

1020</section>

1021<section id="_93">

1022<h3 id="_93_toc">

1023Diagnostic and Staging Evaluation</h3>

1024<p id="_94" tabindex="-1">

1025Clinical evaluation is dependent on laboratory and radiographical imaging tests that include the following: </p>

1026<div class="pdq-content-list">

1027<ul id="_235">

1028<li>

1029 Liver function tests and other laboratory studies.</li>

1030<li>

1031 Abdominal ultrasonography.</li>

1032<li>

1033Computed tomography.</li>

1034<li>

1035Magnetic resonance imaging.</li>

1036<li>

1037Magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography.</li>

1038</ul>

1039</div>

1040<p id="_257" tabindex="-1">

1041These tests demonstrate the extent of the primary tumor and help determine the presence or absence of distant metastases.</p>

1042<p id="_236" tabindex="-1">

1043If a patient is medically fit for surgery and the tumor is amenable to surgical resection, surgical exploration is performed. Pathological examination of the resected specimen is done to establish definitive pathological staging. </p>

1044</section>

1045<section id="_7">

1046<h3 id="_7_toc">

1047Prognosis</h3>

1048<p id="_8" tabindex="-1">

1049Prognosis depends in part on the tumor&#8217;s anatomical location, which affects resectability. Because of proximity to major blood vessels and diffuse extension within the liver, a bile duct tumor can be difficult to resect. Total resection is possible in 25% to 30% of lesions that originate in the distal bile duct; the resectability rate is lower for lesions that occur in more proximal sites.[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

10503</a>

1051] </p>

1052<p id="_151" tabindex="-1">

1053Complete resection with negative surgical margins offers the only chance of cure for bile duct cancer. For localized, resectable extrahepatic and intrahepatic tumors, the presence of involved lymph nodes and perineural invasion are significant adverse prognostic factors.[<a href="#cit/section_1.4">

10544</a>

1055-<a href="#cit/section_1.6">

10566</a>

1057]</p>

1058<p id="_152" tabindex="-1">

1059Additionally, among patients with intrahepatic cholangiocarcinomas, worse outcomes have been associated with the following:[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

10607</a>

1061-<a href="#cit/section_1.9">

10629</a>

1063]</p>

1064<div class="pdq-content-list">

1065<ul id="_237">

1066<li>

1067A personal history of primary sclerosing cholangitis.</li>

1068<li>

1069Elevated cancer antigen 19-9 level.</li>

1070<li>

1071Periductal infiltrating tumor growth pattern.</li>

1072<li>

1073Presence of hepatic venous invasion.</li>

1074</ul>

1075</div>

Evaluation of the extent of disease at laparotomy is an important component of staging.</p>

2718<p id="_271" tabindex="-1">

2719The treatment of bile duct cancer depends primarily on whether the cancer can be completely removed by surgery.</p>

2720<section id="_28">

2721<h3 id="_28_toc">

2722Resectable (Localized) Bile Duct Cancer</h3>

2723<p id="_29" tabindex="-1">

2724Localized intrahepatic and extrahepatic bile duct cancer may be completely removed by surgery. These tumors represent a very small number of cases that are usually in the distal common bile duct. Among patients treated with surgical resection, long-term prognosis varies depending on primary tumor extent, margin status, lymph node involvement, and additional pathological features.[<a href="#cit/section_4.1">

27251</a>

2726,<a href="#cit/section_4.2">

27272</a>

2728]</p>

2729<p id="_238" tabindex="-1">

2730Extended resections of hepatic duct bifurcation tumors (Klatskin tumors, also known as hilar tumors) to include adjacent liver, either by lobectomy or removal of portions of segments 4 and 5 of the liver, may be performed. If major hepatic resection is necessary to achieve a complete resection, postoperative hepatic reserve should be evaluated. For patients with underlying cirrhosis, the Child-Pugh class and the Model for End-Stage Liver Disease score are determined. </p>

2731</section>

2732<section id="_30">

2733<h3 id="_30_toc">

2734Unresectable (Including Metastatic and Recurrent) Bile Duct Cancer</h3>

2735<p id="_265" tabindex="-1">

2736Most cases of intrahepatic, distal, and perihilar bile duct cancer are unresectable and

2737cannot be completely removed. Often the cancer invades directly into the portal vein, the adjacent liver, along the common bile duct, and to adjacent lymph nodes. Portal hypertension may result from invasion of the portal vein. Spread to distant parts of the body is

2738uncommon, but intra-abdominal metastases, particularly peritoneal metastases, do occur. Transperitoneal and hematogenous hepatic metastases also occur

2739with bile duct cancer of all sites. Moreover, most patients who undergo resection will develop recurrent disease within the hepatobiliary system or, less frequently, at distant sites.</p>

2740<p id="_432" tabindex="-1">

2741In locally advanced disease, phase II trials have evaluated chemoradiotherapy with the goal of improved local control and potential downstaging for surgical resection.[<a href="#cit/section_4.3">

27423</a>

2743,<a href="#cit/section_4.4">

27444</a>

2745] These approaches have not been compared with standard therapy, and the curative potential is unknown.</p>

2746<p id="_239" tabindex="-1">

2747For patients with unresectable bile duct cancer, management is directed at palliation.</p>

2886Surgery</h4>

2887<section id="_441">

2888<h5 id="_441_toc">

2889Intrahepatic bile duct cancer</h5>

2890<p id="_442" tabindex="-1">

2891For intrahepatic bile duct cancers, hepatic resection to achieve negative margins is potentially curative. If a major liver resection is necessary to achieve negative surgical margins, preoperative portal vein embolization may be considered to optimize the volume of the remnant liver.</p>

2892<p id="_443" tabindex="-1">

2893Partial liver resection or partial hepatectomy to achieve negative margins is a procedure with curative intent for patients with intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma.[<a href="#cit/section_5.1">

28941</a>

2895] The extent of liver resection necessary depends on the extent of hepatic parenchymal involvement and the proximity of the tumor to major blood vessels in this region.</p>

2896<p id="_466" tabindex="-1">

2897The role of routine portal lymphadenectomy has not been well established because of the risk of common bile duct devascularization. </p>

2898</section>

2899<section id="_467">

2900<h5 id="_467_toc">

2901Perihilar bile duct cancer</h5>

2902<p id="_468" tabindex="-1">

2903For perihilar cholangiocarcinomas (Klatskin tumors), bile duct resection alone leads to high local recurrence rates resulting from the early confluence of the hepatic ducts and the caudate lobe. The addition of partial hepatectomy that includes the caudate lobe has improved long-term outcomes, but it may be associated with increased postoperative complications.[<a href="#cit/section_5.2">

29042</a>

2905] With this aggressive surgical approach, 5-year survival rates of 20% to 50% have been reported.[<a href="#cit/section_5.3">

29063</a>

2907] An understanding of both the normal and varied vascular and ductal anatomy of the porta hepatis has increased the number of hepatic duct bifurcation tumors that can be resected.</p>

2908<p id="_469" tabindex="-1">

2909The primary site of relapse after surgical resection is local; however, distant recurrence is also frequently reported.[<a href="#cit/section_5.4">

29104</a>

2911]</p>

2912<p id="_470" tabindex="-1">

2913The optimal surgical procedure for carcinoma of the perihilar bile duct varies according to the location of the tumor along the biliary tree, the extent of hepatic parenchymal involvement, and the proximity of the tumor to major blood vessels in this region. The state of the regional lymph nodes is assessed at the time of surgery because of their prognostic significance. Operations for bile duct cancer are usually extensive. A historical cohort reported an operative mortality rate of approximately 10%, along with a roughly 40% risk of disease recurrence.[<a href="#cit/section_5.5">

29145</a>

2915]</p>

2916<p id="_471" tabindex="-1">

2917In jaundiced patients, the role of percutaneous transhepatic catheter drainage or endoscopic placement of a stent for relief of biliary obstruction is controversial because of inconsistent findings of significant clinical benefit and concerns of increased risk of postoperative complications.[<a href="#cit/section_5.6">

29186</a>

2919] However, percutaneous transhepatic catheter drainage or endoscopic placement of a stent for relief of biliary obstruction may be considered before surgery, particularly if jaundice is severe or an element of azotemia is present.[<a href="#cit/section_5.7">

29207</a>

2921,<a href="#cit/section_5.8">

29228</a>

2923]</p>

2924</section>

2925<section id="_472">

2926<h5 id="_472_toc">

2927Distal bile duct cancer</h5>

2928<p id="_473" tabindex="-1">

2929Complete surgical resection with negative surgical margins offers the only chance of cure for distal bile duct cancers. Bile duct tumors can be difficult to resect because of their proximity to major blood vessels and diffuse infiltration of adjacent bile ducts. Total resection is possible in 25% to 30% of lesions that originate in the distal bile duct; the resectability rate is lower for lesions that occur in more proximal sites.[<a href="#cit/section_5.9">

29309</a>

2931]</p>

2932<p id="_474" tabindex="-1">

2933The optimum surgical procedure for carcinoma of the distal bile duct will vary according to the location of the tumor along the biliary tree, the

2934extent of hepatic parenchymal involvement, and the proximity of the tumor to

2935major blood vessels in this region. The regional lymph nodes are assessed at the time of surgery because they have prognostic significance. Patients with cancer of the

2936lower end of the duct and regional lymph node involvement may warrant an

2937extensive resection (Whipple procedure). The 5-year survival outcomes range between 20% and 50%.[<a href="#cit/section_5.10">

293810</a>

2939,<a href="#cit/section_5.11">

294011</a>

2941] Bypass operations or endoluminal

2942stents are alternatives if intraoperatively the tumor is found to be unresectable.[<a href="#cit/section_5.10">

294310</a>

2944,<a href="#cit/section_5.11">

294511</a>

2946]</p>

2947<p id="_475" tabindex="-1">

2948In jaundiced patients, the role of percutaneous transhepatic catheter drainage or endoscopic placement of a stent for relief of biliary obstruction is controversial, but these options may be considered before surgery, particularly if jaundice is severe or an element of azotemia is present.[<a href="#cit/section_5.7">

29497</a>

2950,<a href="#cit/section_5.8">

29518</a>

2952]</p>

2958<section id="_477">

2959<h5 id="_477_toc">

2960Chemotherapy</h5>

2961<p id="_478" tabindex="-1">

2962Numerous retrospective series have suggested that adjuvant chemotherapy after complete surgical resection may be beneficial.[<a href="#cit/section_5.12">

296312</a>

2964,<a href="#cit/section_5.13">

296513</a>

2966][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

2970Evidence (chemotherapy):</p>

2971<div class="pdq-content-list">
