371<p id="_13" tabindex="-1">

373affected</a>

374 individual must be evaluated and monitored for occasional individual variations. Prognostic determinants at diagnosis include the stage of the RCC, whether the tumor is confined to the kidney, primary tumor size, Fuhrman nuclear grade, and multifocality.[<a href="#cit/section_1.11">

381<h3 id="_14_toc">

382Family History as a Risk Factor for Renal Cell Carcinoma</h3>

383<p id="_15" tabindex="-1">

384Kidney cancer and renal pelvis cancer account for about 4.2% of all adult malignancies in the United States.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

3851</a>

386] Epidemiological studies of RCC suggest that a family history of RCC is a risk factor for the disease.[<a href="#cit/section_1.4">

3874</a>

388,<a href="#cit/section_1.14">

38914</a>

390,<a href="#cit/section_1.15">

39115</a>

392] An analysis of RCCs diagnosed before the year 2000 in the Sweden Family-Cancer Database included all Swedes born since 1931 and their biological parents. The study observed that risk of RCC was particularly high in the siblings of those with RCC. Siblings of individuals with RCC had a higher relative risk (RR) than parent-child pairs. This suggests that a <a class="definition" type="GlossaryTermRefs" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339339&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000339339&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic'); return(false);">

393recessive</a>

394 gene contributes to the development of sporadic RCC.[<a href="#cit/section_1.14">

39514</a>

396] In another study, investigators studied all patients in Iceland who developed RCC between 1955 and 1999 (1,078 cases). In addition, they used an extensive computerized database to perform a unique genealogical study that included more than 600,000 Icelandic individuals. Results revealed that nearly 60% of Icelandic patients with RCC had a <a class="definition" type="GlossaryTermRefs" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460150&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000460150&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic'); return(false);">

397first-degree relative</a>

398 (FDR) or a <a class="definition" type="GlossaryTermRefs" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=485395&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000485395&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic'); return(false);">

399second-degree relative</a>

400 (SDR) with RCC. Siblings of patients with RCC had an estimated RR of 2.5.[<a href="#cit/section_1.4">

4014</a>

402] A study that evaluated 80,309 monozygotic twins and 123,382 same-sex dizygotic twins in Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden found excess cancer risk in twins whose co-twin was diagnosed with cancer.[<a href="#cit/section_1.15">

40315</a>

404] The estimated cumulative risks were an absolute 5% higher (95% confidence interval [CI], 4%&#8211;6%) in dizygotic twins (37%; 95% CI, 36%&#8211;38%) and an absolute 14% higher (95% CI, 12%&#8211;16%) in monozygotic twins (46%; 95% CI, 44%&#8211;48%)&#8212;for twins whose co-twin also developed cancer&#8212;than that in the overall cohort (32%). Overall <a class="definition" type="GlossaryTermRefs" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781848&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000781848&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic'); return(false);">

405heritability</a>

406 of cancer, calculated by assessing the relative contribution of heredity versus shared environment, was estimated to be 33%. Kidney cancer heritability was estimated to be 38% (95% CI, 21%&#8211;55%). Shared environmental factors did not significantly contribute to overall risk.</p>

407<p id="_16" tabindex="-1">

408 Young age at RCC onset is also a clue that hereditary etiology is possible. In contrast with sporadic RCC, which is generally diagnosed during the fifth to seventh decades of life, hereditary forms of RCC are generally diagnosed at an earlier age. In a review of over 600 cases of hereditary RCC from the National Cancer Institute, the median age of RCC diagnosis was 37 years, with 70% of cases being diagnosed at age 46 years or younger.[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

4093</a>

410] This is lower than the median age of RCC diagnosis in the general population, which is 64 years.[<a href="#cit/section_1.16">

41116</a>

412] Heritable RCCs are often multifocal and bilateral. A retrospective analysis of 1,235 patients with RCC who underwent genetic testing revealed that 6.1% of this population had positive genetic test results, 75.5% had negative test results, and 18.4% had a <a class="definition" type="GlossaryTermRefs" href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=556493&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000556493&amp;version=healthprofessional&amp;language=English&amp;dictionary=genetic'); return(false);">

413variant of unknown significance</a>

414. Young age at RCC diagnosis was the only variable associated with a positive test result.[<a href="#cit/section_1.8">

4158</a>

416] Other series showed that patients with non-clear cell advanced RCC may have an enrichment for pathogenic variants when compared with patients who had clear cell RCC; however, current research data are limited.[<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

4175</a>

418,<a href="#cit/section_1.17">

41917</a>

420]</p>
