906General Information About Childhood Central Nervous System (CNS) Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor</h2>

939<p id="_136" tabindex="-1">

940Primary brain tumors, including atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumors, are a diverse group of diseases that together constitute the most common solid tumor of childhood. The PDQ childhood brain tumor treatment summaries are primarily organized according to the World Health Organization classification of nervous system tumors.[<a href="#cit/section_1.1">

9411</a>

942,<a href="#cit/section_1.2">

9432</a>

944] Brain tumors are classified according to histology, but immunohistochemical analysis, cytogenetic and molecular genetic findings, and measures of mitotic activity are increasingly used in tumor diagnosis and classification. Tumor location and extent of spread are important factors that affect treatment and prognosis. For a description of the classification of nervous system tumors and a link to the corresponding treatment summary for each type of brain tumor, see <a href="/types/brain/hp/child-brain-treatment-pdq">

945Childhood Brain and Spinal Cord Tumors Treatment Overview</a>

946.</p>

947<p id="_139" tabindex="-1">

948CNS atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumor (AT/RT) is a rare, clinically aggressive tumor that most often affects children aged 3 years and younger but can occur in older children and adults. Approximately one-half of AT/RTs arise in the posterior fossa.[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

9493</a>

950] The diagnostic evaluation includes magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the neuraxis and lumbar cerebrospinal fluid examination. AT/RT has been linked to somatic and germline mutations of <em class="gene-name">

951SMARCB1</em>

952 and, less commonly, <em class="gene-name">

953SMARCA4</em>

954, both of which are tumor suppressor genes.[<a href="#cit/section_1.4">

9554</a>

956] There is no current standard treatment for children with AT/RT. Multimodality treatment consisting of surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy is under evaluation. </p>

957<p id="_82" tabindex="-1">

958Based on present biological understanding, AT/RT is part of a larger family of rhabdoid tumors. In this summary, the term AT/RT refers to CNS tumors only and the term rhabdoid tumor reflects the possibility of both CNS and non-CNS tumors. Unless specifically noted in the text, this summary is referring to CNS AT/RT.</p>

959<p id="_1796" tabindex="-1">

960Childhood and adolescent cancer survivors require close monitoring because side effects of cancer therapy may persist or develop months or years after treatment. For specific information about the incidence, type, and monitoring of late effects in childhood and adolescent cancer survivors, see <a href="/types/childhood-cancers/late-effects-hp-pdq">

961Late Effects of Treatment for Childhood Cancer</a>

962.</p>

963<section id="_41">

964<h3 id="_41_toc">

965Incidence </h3>

966<p id="_42" tabindex="-1">

967The exact incidence of childhood CNS AT/RT is difficult to determine because the tumor is rare and has only been recognized since 1996.[<a href="#cit/section_1.5">

9685</a>

969] </p>

970<div class="pdq-content-list">

971<ul id="_142">

972<li>

973In two North American prospective studies performed by the Children&#8217;s Cancer Group and the Pediatric Oncology Group for children aged 3 years or younger at diagnosis, retrospective review disclosed that approximately 10% of children with brain tumors had AT/RTs.[<a href="#cit/section_1.6">

9746</a>

975]</li>

976<li>

977A Taiwanese study found that AT/RTs account for 26% of primitive or embryonal tumors in children younger than 3 years.[<a href="#cit/section_1.7">

9787</a>

979]</li>

980<li>

981The Austrian Brain Tumor Registry (recruitment period, 1996&#8211;2006) confirmed that AT/RTs represented the sixth most common malignant brain tumor among 311 newly diagnosed children (6.1%), with a peak incidence during the first 2 years of life.[<a href="#cit/section_1.8">

9828</a>

983] </li>

984</ul>

985</div>

986<p id="_143" tabindex="-1">

987The incidence in older patients is unknown. However, in the Central Nervous System Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor Registry (AT/RT Registry), 12 of the 42 patients (29%) were older than 36 months at the time of diagnosis.[<a href="#cit/section_1.9">

9889</a>

989] </p>

1004<section id="_43">

1005<h3 id="_43_toc">

1006Clinical Presentation</h3>

1007<p id="_44" tabindex="-1">

1008Childhood AT/RT is a clinically aggressive tumor that primarily occurs in children younger than 3 years, but it also can occur in older children and adults.[<a href="#cit/section_1.10">

100910</a>

1010,<a href="#cit/section_1.11">

101111</a>

1012] </p>

1013<p id="_140" tabindex="-1">

1014Approximately one-half of all AT/RTs arise in the posterior fossa, although it can occur anywhere in the CNS.[<a href="#cit/section_1.3">

10153</a>

1016,<a href="#cit/section_1.6">

10176</a>

1018] Tumors of the posterior fossa may occur in the cerebellopontine angle or more midline. Involvement of individual cranial nerves has been noted. </p>

1019<p id="_46" tabindex="-1">

1020 Because AT/RT grows rapidly, patients typically have a fairly short history of progressive symptoms, measured in days to weeks. Signs and symptoms depend on tumor location. Young patients with posterior fossa tumors usually present with symptoms related to hydrocephalus, which include the following:</p>

1021<div class="pdq-content-list">

1022<ul id="_144">

1023<li>

1024Early-morning headaches.</li>

1025<li>

1026Vomiting.</li>

1027<li>

1028Lethargy.</li>

1029<li>

1030Increased head circumference.</li>

1031</ul>

1032</div>

1033<p id="_145" tabindex="-1">

1034They may also develop ataxia or regression of motor skills.</p>

1035<p id="_112" tabindex="-1">

1036Registry data suggest that up to 30% of patients present with disseminated disease.[<a href="#cit/section_1.9">

10379</a>

1038,<a href="#cit/section_1.12">

103912</a>

1040,<a href="#cit/section_1.13">

104113</a>

1042] Dissemination is typically through leptomeningeal pathways seeding the spine and other areas of the brain. Up to 35% of patients present with germline mutations and may be prone to synchronous, multifocal tumors. There are also rare reports of patients with synchronous renal rhabdoid tumors and CNS AT/RTs.[<a href="#cit/section_1.14">

104314</a>

1044-<a href="#cit/section_1.17">

104517</a>

1046]</p>

1049<h3 id="_113_toc">

1050Diagnostic Evaluation</h3>

1051<p id="_114" tabindex="-1">

1052All patients with suspected childhood AT/RT should undergo MRI of the brain and spine. Unless medically contraindicated, the lumbar cerebrospinal fluid should be inspected for evidence of tumor. Patients may also undergo renal ultrasonography to detect synchronous tumors. </p>

1053<p id="_146" tabindex="-1">

1054 AT/RTs cannot be reliably distinguished from other malignant brain tumors on the basis of clinical history or radiographic evaluation alone. Surgery is necessary to obtain tissue and confirm the diagnosis. Immunohistochemical staining for loss of SMARCB1 protein expression is also used to confirm the diagnosis.[<a href="#cit/section_1.18">

1056,<a href="#cit/section_1.19">

1058]</p>

1292<p id="_20" tabindex="-1">

1293Childhood central nervous system (CNS) atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumor (AT/RT) was first described as a discrete clinical entity in 1987 [<a href="#cit/section_2.1">

12941</a>

1295] on the basis of its distinctive pathological and genetic characteristics. Before then, it was most often classified as a medulloblastoma, CNS primitive neuroectodermal tumor (CNS PNET), or choroid plexus carcinoma. The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies AT/RT as an embryonal grade IV neoplasm.[<a href="#cit/section_2.2">

12962</a>

1297]</p>

1298<p id="_78" tabindex="-1">

1299Histologically, AT/RT is morphologically heterogeneous, typically containing sheets of large epithelioid cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and scattered rhabdoid cells, most often with accompanying components of primitive neuroectodermal cells (small round blue cells), mesenchymal cells, and/or glial cells.[<a href="#cit/section_2.3">

13003</a>

1301] </p>

1302<p id="_151" tabindex="-1">

1303Immunohistochemical staining for epithelial markers (cytokeratin or epithelial membrane antigen), glial fibrillary acidic protein, synaptophysin (or neurofilament), and smooth muscle (desmin) may help to identify the heterogeneity of differentiation, but will vary depending on the cellular composition.[<a href="#cit/section_2.4">

13044</a>

1305] Rhabdoid cells, while not present in all AT/RTs, will express vimentin, epithelial membrane antigen, and smooth muscle actin.</p>

1306<p id="_117" tabindex="-1">

1307Immunohistochemical staining for the SMARCB1 protein is useful in establishing the diagnosis of AT/RT. A loss of SMARCB1 staining is noted in neoplastic cells, but staining is retained in non-neoplastic cells (e.g., vascular endothelial cells).[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

13085</a>

1309-<a href="#cit/section_2.7">

13107</a>

1311]</p>

1312<p id="_152" tabindex="-1">

1313AT/RT is a rapidly growing tumor that can have an MIB-1 labeling index of 50% to 100%.[<a href="#cit/section_2.8">

13148</a>

1315] </p>

1317<h3 id="_162_toc">

1318Genomics of CNS Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor (AT/RT)</h3>

1319<section id="_sm_CDR0000779375_148">

1320<h4 id="_sm_CDR0000779375_148_toc">

1321<em class="gene-name">

1322SMARCB1</em>

1323 and <em class="gene-name">

1324SMARCA4</em>

1325 genes</h4>

1326<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_21" tabindex="-1">

1327 AT/RT was the first primary pediatric brain tumor in which a candidate tumor suppressor gene, <em class="gene-name">

1328SMARCB1</em>

1329 (previously known as <em class="gene-name">

1330INI1</em>

1331 and <em class="gene-name">

1332hSNF5</em>

1333), was identified.[<a href="#cit/section_2.9">

13349</a>

1335] <em class="gene-name">

1336SMARCB1</em>

1337 is genomically altered in most rhabdoid tumors, including CNS, renal, and extrarenal rhabdoid malignancies.[<a href="#cit/section_2.9">

13389</a>

1339] <em class="gene-name">

1340SMARCB1</em>

1341 is a component of a SWItch/Sucrose Non-Fermentable (SWI/SNF) chromatin-remodeling complex.[<a href="#cit/section_2.10">

134210</a>

1343]</p>

1344<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_118" tabindex="-1">

1345 Rare cases of rhabdoid tumors expressing SMARCB1 and lacking <em class="gene-name">

1346SMARCB1</em>

1347 mutations have also been associated with somatic or germline mutations of <em class="gene-name">

1348SMARCA4/BRG1</em>

1349, another member of the SWI/SNF chromatin-remodeling complex.[<a href="#cit/section_2.7">

13507</a>

1351,<a href="#cit/section_2.11">

135211</a>

1353,<a href="#cit/section_2.12">

135412</a>

1355]</p>

1356<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_171" tabindex="-1">

1357Less commonly, <em class="gene-name">

1358SMARCA4</em>

1359-negative (with retained <em class="gene-name">

1360SMARCB1</em>

1361) tumors have been described.[<a href="#cit/section_2.7">

13627</a>

1363,<a href="#cit/section_2.11">

136411</a>

1365,<a href="#cit/section_2.12">

136612</a>

1367] Loss of SMARCB1 or SMARCA4 staining is a defining marker for AT/RT. </p>

1368<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_161" tabindex="-1">

1369The 2016 WHO classification defines AT/RT by the presence of either <em class="gene-name">

1370SMARCB1</em>

1371 or <em class="gene-name">

1372SMARCA4</em>

1373 alterations. Tumors with histological features of AT/RT that lack these genomic alterations are termed <em>

1374CNS embryonal tumor with rhabdoid features</em>

1375.[<a href="#cit/section_2.2">

13762</a>

1377]</p>

1378<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_156" tabindex="-1">

1379Despite the absence of recurring genomic alterations beyond <em class="gene-name">

1380SMARCB1</em>

1381 and <em class="gene-name">

1382SMARCA4</em>

1383,[<a href="#cit/section_2.13">

138413</a>

1385-<a href="#cit/section_2.15">

138615</a>

1387] biologically distinctive subsets of AT/RT have been identified.[<a href="#cit/section_2.16">

138816</a>

1389-<a href="#cit/section_2.18">

139018</a>

1391] The following three distinctive subsets of AT/RT were identified through the use of DNA methylation arrays for 150 AT/RT tumors and gene expression arrays for 67 AT/RT tumors:[<a href="#cit/section_2.17">

139217</a>

1393]</p>

1394<div class="pdq-content-list">

1395<ul id="_sm_CDR0000779375_157">

1396<li>

1397<strong>

1398AT/RT TYR:</strong>

1399 This subset represented approximately one-third of cases and was characterized by elevated expression of melanosomal markers such as <em class="gene-name">

1400TYR</em>

1401 (the gene encoding tyrosinase). Cases in this subset were primarily infratentorial, with most presenting in children aged 0 to 1 year and showing chromosome 22q loss.[<a href="#cit/section_2.17">

140217</a>

1403] For patients with AT/RT TYR, the mean overall survival (OS) was 37 months in a clinically heterogeneous group (95% confidence interval [CI], 18&#8211;56 months).[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

140419</a>

1405] In the prospective European Rhabdoid Registry (EU-RHAB) series, patients aged 1 year and older with an AT/RT-TYR subgroup designation demonstrated a 5-year OS rate of 71%, while those younger than 1 year with a non-TYR subgroup designation had a very poor survival rate.[<a href="#cit/section_2.20">

140620</a>

1407]</li>

1408<li>

1409<strong>

1410AT/RT SHH:</strong>

1411 This subset represented approximately 40% of cases and was characterized by elevated expression of genes in the sonic hedgehog (SHH) pathway (e.g., <em class="gene-name">

1412GLI2</em>

1413 and <em class="gene-name">

1414MYCN</em>

1415). Cases in this subset occurred with similar frequency in the supratentorium and infratentorium. While most patients presented before the age of 2 years, approximately one-third of patients presented between the ages of 2 and 5 years.[<a href="#cit/section_2.17">

141617</a>

1417] For patients with AT/RT SHH, the mean OS was 16 months (95% CI, 8&#8211;25 months).[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

141819</a>

1419]</li>

1420<li>

1421<strong>

1422AT/RT MYC:</strong>

1423 This subset represented approximately one-fourth of cases and was characterized by elevated expression of MYC. AT/RT MYC cases tended to occur in the supratentorial compartment. While most AT/RT MYC cases occurred by the age of 5 years, AT/RT MYC represented the most common subset diagnosed at age 6 years and older. Focal deletions of <em class="gene-name">

1424SMARCB1</em>

1425 were the most common mechanism of SMARCB1 loss for this subset.[<a href="#cit/section_2.17">

142617</a>

1427] For patients with AT/RT MYC, the mean OS was 13 months (95% CI, 5&#8211;22 months).[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

142819</a>

1429]</li>

1430</ul>

1431</div>

1432<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_172" tabindex="-1">

1433Cribriform neuroepithelial tumor is a brain cancer that also presents in young children and has genomic and epigenomic characteristics that are very similar to AT/RT TYR.[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

143419</a>

1435] For more information, see the <a href="/types/brain/hp/child-cns-atrt-treatment-pdq#_171">

1436Cribriform Neuroepithelial Tumor</a>

1437 section.</p>

1438<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_80" tabindex="-1">

1439In addition to somatic mutations, germline mutations in <em class="gene-name">

1440SMARCB1</em>

1441 have been reported in a substantial subset of patients with AT/RT.[<a href="#cit/section_2.9">

14429</a>

1443,<a href="#cit/section_2.21">

144421</a>

1445] A study of 65 children with rhabdoid tumors found that 23 (35%) had germline mutations and/or deletions of <em class="gene-name">

1446SMARCB1</em>

1447.[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

14485</a>

1449] Children with germline alterations in <em class="gene-name">

1450SMARCB1</em>

1451 presented at an earlier age than did sporadic cases (median age, approximately 5 months vs. 18 months) and were more likely to present with synchronous, multifocal tumors.[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

14525</a>

1453] One parent was found to be a carrier of the <em class="gene-name">

1454SMARCB1</em>

1455 germline abnormality in 7 of 22 evaluated cases showing germline alterations, with four of the carrier parents being unaffected by <em class="gene-name">

1456SMARCB1</em>

1457-associated cancers.[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

14585</a>

1459] This indicates that AT/RT shows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern with incomplete penetrance.</p>

1460<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_141" tabindex="-1">

1461Gonadal mosaicism has also been observed, as evidenced by families in which multiple siblings are affected by AT/RT and have identical <em class="gene-name">

1462SMARCB1</em>

1463 alterations, but both parents lack a <em class="gene-name">

1464SMARCB1</em>

1465 mutation/deletion.[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

14665</a>

1467,<a href="#cit/section_2.6">

14686</a>

1469] Screening for germline <em class="gene-name">

1470SMARCB1</em>

1471 mutations in children diagnosed with AT/RT is suggested for counseling families on the genetic implications of their child&#8217;s AT/RT diagnosis.[<a href="#cit/section_2.5">

14725</a>

1473] Preliminary recommendations for the genetic evaluation and subsequent presymptomatic screening of nonaffected mutation carriers (including parents and siblings of affected patients) have been reported and are likely to evolve as the understanding of rhabdoid tumor predisposition improves.[<a href="#cit/section_2.22">

147422</a>

1475] In patients with a predisposition to AT/RT, whole-body MRI may help to identify synchronous rhabdoid tumors outside of the CNS.</p>

1476<p id="_sm_CDR0000779375_169" tabindex="-1">

1477Loss of SMARCB1 or SMARCA4 protein expression has therapeutic significance, because this loss creates a dependence of the cancer cells on EZH2 activity.[<a href="#cit/section_2.23">

147823</a>

1479] Preclinical studies have shown that some AT/RT xenograft lines with <em class="gene-name">

1480SMARCB1</em>

1481 loss respond to EZH2 inhibitors with tumor growth inhibition and occasional tumor regression.[<a href="#cit/section_2.24">

148224</a>

1483,<a href="#cit/section_2.25">

148425</a>

1485] In a study of the EZH2 inhibitor tazemetostat, objective responses were observed in adult patients whose tumors had either <em class="gene-name">

1486SMARCB1</em>

1487 or <em class="gene-name">

1488SMARCA4</em>

1489 loss (non-CNS malignant rhabdoid tumors and epithelioid sarcoma).[<a href="#cit/section_2.26">

149026</a>

1491] For more information, see the <a href="/types/brain/hp/child-cns-atrt-treatment-pdq#_55">

1492Treatment of Recurrent Childhood CNS Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor</a>

1493 section.</p>

1497<h3 id="_50_toc">

1498Rhabdoid Tumor Predisposition Syndrome (RTPS)</h3>

1499<p id="_84" tabindex="-1">

1500RTPS, which is primarily related to germline <em class="gene-name">

1501SMARCB1</em>

1502 alterations (and less commonly to germline <em class="gene-name">

1503SMARCA4</em>

1504 alterations), has been clearly defined.[<a href="#cit/section_2.9">

15059</a>

1506,<a href="#cit/section_2.21">

150721</a>

1508] RTPS is highly suggested in patients with synchronous occurrence of extracranial malignant rhabdoid tumor (kidney or soft tissue) and AT/RT, bilateral malignant rhabdoid tumors of the kidney, or malignant rhabdoid tumors in two or more siblings. </p>

1509<p id="_119" tabindex="-1">

1510This syndrome is manifested by a marked predisposition to the development of malignant rhabdoid tumors in infancy and early childhood. Up to one-third of AT/RTs are thought to arise in the setting of RTPS, and most of these occur within the first year of life. The most common non-CNS malignancy of RTPS is malignant rhabdoid tumor of the kidney, which is also noted in infancy.</p>

1511<p id="_120" tabindex="-1">

1512For more information about RTPS, see the <a href="/types/kidney/hp/wilms-treatment-pdq#_1105">

1513Genetic Testing and Surveillance of Rhabdoid Tumors of the Kidney</a>

1514 section in Wilms Tumor and Other Childhood Kidney Tumors Treatment.</p>

1517<h3 id="_171_toc">

1518Cribriform Neuroepithelial Tumor</h3>

1519<p id="_172" tabindex="-1">

1520Cribriform neuroepithelial tumor is histologically and clinically distinct from AT/RT, but it has genomic and epigenomic characteristics that are very similar to AT/RT TYR.[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

152119</a>

1522] Like AT/RT, cribriform neuroepithelial tumor occurs in young children (median age, 1&#8211;2 years) and tumor cells lack SMARCB1 expression. Histologically, cribriform neuroepithelial tumor is characterized by the presence of cribriform strands and ribbons, but there is an absence of rhabdoid tumor cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm. Like AT/RT TYR, tyrosinase expression is commonly observed. The outcome of patients with cribriform neuroepithelial tumor is more favorable than the outcome of patients with AT/RT TYR. In one study, only one death was reported among ten children with cribriform neuroepithelial tumor.[<a href="#cit/section_2.19">

152319</a>

1672<p id="_31" tabindex="-1">

1673A standard treatment for children with newly diagnosed central nervous system (CNS) atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumor (AT/RT) has not yet been defined. Given the highly aggressive nature of the tumor, most patients have been treated with intensive multimodality therapy. However, the extent of treatment, particularly for radiation therapy, is limited because of the young age of most patients. </p>

1685<h3 id="_125_toc">

1686Surgery, Chemotherapy, and Radiation Therapy (Multimodality Therapy)</h3>

1687<p id="_121" tabindex="-1">

1688The extent of the surgical resection may affect survival. Data from the Central Nervous System Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor Registry (AT/RT Registry) suggest that patients who have had a complete resection may have a longer median survival, although complete surgical resection is often difficult because of the invasive nature of the tumor.[<a href="#cit/section_4.1">

16891</a>

1690] </p>

1691<p id="_32" tabindex="-1">

1692Chemotherapy has been the main adjuvant therapy for very young children with AT/RT. Cooperative group studies that included children younger than 36 months demonstrated poor survival when treated with standard chemotherapeutic regimens alone.[<a href="#cit/section_4.2">

16932</a>

1694] The Children&#8217;s Cancer Group reported a 2-year event-free survival (EFS) rate of 14% for 28 children younger than 36 months who were treated with multiagent chemotherapy.[<a href="#cit/section_4.3">

16953</a>

1696]</p>

1697<p id="_126" tabindex="-1">

1698 Intensive regimens that use varying combinations of high-dose chemotherapy,[<a href="#cit/section_4.4">

16994</a>

1700][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1701Level of evidence C1</a>

1702]; [<a href="#cit/section_4.5">

17035</a>

1704,<a href="#cit/section_4.6">

17056</a>

1706][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810037&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1707Level of evidence C2</a>

1708] intrathecal chemotherapy, and radiation therapy have led to prolonged survival for some patients.</p>

1709<p id="_1839" tabindex="-1">

1710 Thirteen patients in the AT/RT Registry were treated with high-dose chemotherapy with hematopoietic stem cell rescue as part of initial therapy.[<a href="#cit/section_4.1">

17111</a>

1712] Four of these patients, two of whom also received radiation, were alive without progressive disease 21.5 to 90 months after diagnosis at last report. Of 15 evaluable children (all younger than 32 months at diagnosis) who were on a chemotherapy Head Start III protocol, 2 survived for more than 47 months.[<a href="#cit/section_4.7">

17137</a>

1714][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1715Level of evidence C1</a>

1716]</p>

1717<p id="_127" tabindex="-1">

1718Radiation therapy appears to have a positive impact on survival for patients with AT/RT.</p>

1719<p id="_1821" tabindex="-1">

1720Evidence (radiation therapy):</p>

1721<div class="pdq-content-list">

1722<ol id="_1822">

1723<li>

1724Of the 42 patients in the AT/RT Registry, 13 (31%) received radiation therapy in addition to chemotherapy as part of their primary therapy.[<a href="#cit/section_4.1">

17251</a>

1726] The radiation field was to the primary tumor bed in nine children, and the radiation field was to the tumor bed and the craniospinal axis in four children. <div class="pdq-content-list">

1727<ul id="_1835">

1728<li>

1729The median survival of these patients was 48 months, while the median survival of all patients on the registry was 16.75 months.</li>

1730</ul>

1731</div>

1732</li>

1733<li>

1734In a retrospective series of 31 patients with AT/RT from St. Jude Children's Research Hospital, the following results were reported:[<a href="#cit/section_4.8">

17358</a>

1736]<div class="pdq-content-list">

1737<ul id="_1836">

1738<li>

1739The 2-year EFS rate for patients older than 3 years was 78%, which was considerably better than the EFS rate for patients younger than 3 years (11%).</li>

1740<li>

1741All but one of the surviving patients (seven of eight) in the older group received craniospinal irradiation and intensive chemotherapy with hematopoietic stem cell transplant.</li>

1742<li>

1743Only 3 of the 22 younger patients received any form of radiation therapy, 2 of whom are disease free.</li>

1744</ul>

1745</div>

1746</li>

1747<li>

1748In a Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) Program registry review, radiation therapy was associated with improved survival in children younger than 3 years.[<a href="#cit/section_4.9">

17499</a>

1750]</li>

1751<li>

1752In the European Registry for rhabdoid tumors series, the following results were observed:[<a href="#cit/section_4.10">

175310</a>

1754][<a href="/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English" onclick="javascript:popWindow('defbyid','CDR0000810035&amp;version=HealthProfessional&amp;language=English'); return(false);">

1755Level of evidence C1</a>

1756]<div class="pdq-content-list">

1757<ul id="_1837">

1758<li>

1759Radiation therapy was also associated with an improved survival, with a 6-year overall survival (OS) rate of 66% (&#177; 0.1%) in irradiated patients.</li>

1760<li>

1761The significant benefit of radiation therapy was corroborated in an extension of this series.[<a href="#cit/section_4.11">

176211</a>

1763]</li>

1764</ul>

1765</div>

1766 </li>

1767</ol>

1768</div>

1769<p id="_128" tabindex="-1">

1770Evidence (multimodality therapy):</p>

1771<div class="pdq-content-list">

1772<ol id="_129">

1773<li>

1774The third Intergroup Rhabdomyosarcoma Study (IRS-III) used radiation therapy, intrathecal methotrexate, cytarabine, hydrocortisone, and systemic multiagent chemotherapy. The results of small retrospective series were encouraging,[<a href="#cit/section_4.12">

177512</a>

1776,<a href="#cit/section_4.13">

177713</a>

1778] leading to the first prospective study of multimodality treatment in this group of patients. </li>

1779<li>

1780On the basis of the previous pilot series, a prospective multi-institutional trial was conducted for children with newly diagnosed CNS AT/RT. Treatment was divided into five phases: preirradiation, chemoradiation, consolidation, maintenance, and continuation therapy. Intrathecal chemotherapy was administered, alternating intralumbar and intraventricular routes. Radiation therapy was either focal (54 Gy) or craniospinal (36 Gy, plus primary boost), depending on the child's age and extent of disease at diagnosis.[<a href="#cit/section_4.14">

178114</a>

1782] <div class="pdq-content-list">

1783<ul id="_130">

1784<li>

1785The 2-year progression-free survival rate was 53% (&#177; 13%), and the 2-year OS rate was 70% (&#177; 10%).</li>

1786<li>

1787Results were most favorable for children who were older, had a gross-total resection, and had no metastatic disease at presentation.</li>

1788<li>

1789Six of the eight children without progressive disease at the time of the report had received conformal radiation therapy, and two children had received craniospinal radiation therapy; seven children had a gross-total resection, and only one child had metastatic disease (this child had persistent, stable disease 1.5 years from diagnosis).</li>

1790</ul>

1791</div>

1792</li>

1793<li>

1794The Children&#8217;s Oncology Group performed a prospective single-arm study of 65 children. Fifty-four of the children were younger than 36 months and received two courses of methotrexate, cyclophosphamide, cisplatin, and etoposide followed by three courses of high-dose carboplatin and thiotepa supported by peripheral stem cell rescue. For patients with nondisseminated disease, focal involved-field radiation therapy was mandated after either induction or consolidation, depending on age. For patients with disseminated disease, craniospinal radiation at the end of therapy was recommended but not mandated.[<a href="#cit/section_4.15">

179515</a>

1796]<div class="pdq-content-list">

1797<ul id="_1834">

1798<li>

1799For all patients, the 4-year EFS rate was 37%, and the 4-year OS rate was 43%.</li>

1800<li>

1801 For children younger than 36 months at diagnosis, the 4-year EFS rate was 35%, compared with 6.4% in a historical cohort of patients who received chemotherapy alone (<em>

1802P</em>

1803 &lt; .0005).</li>

1804<li>

1805For the 11 children aged 36 months or older at diagnosis, the 4-year EFS rate was 48%, and the 4-year OS rate was 57%.</li>

1806<li>

1807 Toxicity from this regimen was significant. Four treatment-related deaths (6% of the patients) were reported, which resulted from sepsis, respiratory failure, or CNS necrosis.</li>

1808</ul>

1809</div>

1810</li>

1811</ol>

1812</div>

1813<p id="_1833" tabindex="-1">

1814On the basis of the two prospective studies summarized above, multimodality therapy with surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy seems to be the best treatment to optimize the survival of children with AT/RT. However, toxicities can be significant, and the most effective regimen and the optimal sequencing of therapies still need to be determined.</p>

1902 <h2>

1903Treatment of Recurrent Childhood CNS Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor</h2>

1904

1905 <div id="_section_55" class="pdq-sections">

1906<nav class="in-this-section" role="navigation">

1907<h6>

1908In This Section</h6>

1909<ul>

1910<li>

1911<a href="#_166_toc">

1912Treatment Options Under Clinical Evaluation</a>

1913</li>

1914</ul>

1915</nav>

1916<p id="_56" tabindex="-1">

1917There is no standard treatment for children with recurrent central nervous system (CNS) atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumor (AT/RT).</p>

1918<p id="_1840" tabindex="-1">

1919 Trials of molecularly targeted therapy are ongoing. In a study of the EZH2 inhibitor tazemetostat in adult patients with epithelioid sarcoma and non-CNS malignant rhabdoid tumors with <em class="gene-name">

1920SMARCB1</em>

1921 or <em class="gene-name">

1922SMARCA4</em>

1923 loss, prolonged stable disease and objective responses were observed.[<a href="#cit/section_5.1">

19241</a>

1925] The activity of tazemetostat in children with AT/RT is under clinical evaluation.</p>

1926<p id="_1841" tabindex="-1">

1927Stereotactic radiation therapy/radiosurgery or focal radiation therapy can also be considered for the treatment of children with recurrent disease.[<a href="#cit/section_5.2">

19282</a>

1929]</p>

1930<p id="_1826" tabindex="-1">

1931Patients or families who desire additional disease-directed therapy should consider entering trials of novel therapeutic approaches because no standard agents have demonstrated clinically significant activity.</p>

1932<p id="_1829" tabindex="-1">

1933 Regardless of whether a decision is made to pursue disease-directed therapy at the time of progression, palliative care remains a central focus of management. This ensures that quality of life is maximized while attempting to reduce symptoms and stress related to the terminal illness.</p>
